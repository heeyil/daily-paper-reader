# How Much of the Routing Gap Is Real? Decomposing the Router-to-Oracle Gap into Reproducible Specialist Advantage and Single-Draw Label Noise 

Teng-Ruei Chen 

Abstract —Routing among large language models (LLMs) promises better quality at lower cost, and a central motivation of the field is the large reported gap between learned routers and a per-instance oracle. However, that oracle is computed from a single correctness label per (query, model), so under stochastic decoding it is one Bernoulli draw rather than a re-producible property. This work recasts the question structurally: the expected per-instance oracle decomposes, Oexp = Orepro +∆ ,into reproducible single-commit headroom Orepro (the ceiling a clairvoyant oracle-weight router can reach) and a single-commit selection floor ∆ = Oexp − Orepro ≥ 0. The main result of this work is a recoverability asymmetry : this floor is closed by no single-commit router—deterministic or randomized—yet is provably recovered by test-time sampling [1], [2] (at the oracle’s own K-draw budget, best-of-K on the committed best model already weakly dominates the independent-pool single-draw oracle, and the general-coupling oracle as n → ∞ ). The selection cap needs no cross-model independence, pinning “not recoverable” to single-commit selection rather than to information in general; this work proves it together with the exact non-negative decomposition and analytic noise-share bounds that collapse as the sampling budget grows. The procedure introduces no new router, only resampling. The magnitude of the floor is a prospective, conservative localiza-tion, not an audit: the primary target LLMRouterBench ( 33 mod-els, 391 ,645 instances) defines its oracle—in its own aggregator code and Sec. 3.4—as a per-query union over single generations at temperature 0.2, so the reported ∼20 -point gap is by construction 

a union of stochastic single draws; but Orepro is non-identifiable from the released k=1 matrix, so we estimate the noise share of the gap by fresh T =0 .2, k ≥ 20 resampling under one-sided, dependence- and guessing-floor-corrected bounds, reframing the “model-recall failure” diagnosis as thin-support union inflation. RouterBench (decoding undisclosed) is a secondary illustration. On a controlled open-model re-generation, single-draw noise accounts for a substantial minority of the router-to-oracle gap— larger on an unsaturated benchmark than on a saturated one, and approaching half on the hardest queries where no model is reliable—while the majority remains recoverable specialist advantage (quantified in the body). This work releases a multi-sample oracle evaluation protocol that routing benchmarks can adopt. 

Index Terms —LLM routing, model selection, evaluation methodology, oracle bias, stochastic decoding, benchmark reli-ability.

# SERVING large language models (LLMs) at scale forces a cost–quality trade-off, and selecting or combining among models per query has become the principal means of navigat-ing it. Three paradigms have emerged: routing (commit to one model up front), cascading (escalate from cheap to expensive), and fusion (run several and aggregate). All rest on a shared premise of model complementarity —no single model is best on every input [3], [4], [5], [6]. This premise is operationalized through a per-instance ora-cle : the model that is best on each query, chosen in hindsight. RouterBench [7] and, at far larger scale, LLMRouterBench [8] report the gap between deployed routers and this oracle as the field’s central measure of remaining opportunity. That gap—and its diagnosis as “model-recall failure”, the router’s failure to select the lone correct specialist—now motivates a growing literature, from preference-trained routers [4] to unified routing–cascading theory [5] and sub-task routing [9]. Yet the oracle is computed from a single correctness label per (query, model). Under stochastic decoding (temperature 

T > 0), that label is one Bernoulli sample of a latent suc-cess probability, so the oracle—a maximum over many such single draws—inherits the upward bias that inflates best-of-N

estimates [10], [11], [12], [13]. Concurrent work has begun to correct this oracle inflation by debiasing it [14]; this work instead asks which part of the routing gap is irrecoverable —a reproducible ceiling that no single-commit router can pass— versus recoverable only by test-time resampling, and proves a 

recoverability asymmetry the debiasing view does not address. As a concrete example, a pool of 10 models that each solve a query with probability 0.1 yields a single-draw oracle of 

1−0.910 ≈ 0.65 , yet no model is reliable and a single-commit router can reach only 0.1: of the apparent 0.65 , only 0.1 is recoverable and 0.55 is single-draw noise (Fig. 2). This paper asks exactly that. We re-estimate per-(query, model) correctness as a probability from k ≥ 10 samples, define an expected and a reproducible oracle, and decompose the router-to-oracle gap into recoverable specialist advantage and single-draw label noise; Fig. 1 previews how the proofs and the experiments fit together.

II. R ELATED WORK 

We organize prior work along two axes: multi-LLM systems that exploit complementarity, and the evaluation ceilings used to judge them. 

Multi-LLM systems. The cost–quality view descends from API-selection and cascading [5], [16]. Preference-trained bi-nary routers [4], [17], unified routing–cascading [5], and sub-task routing [9] select among models, whereas Mixture-of-Agents [3] and compound multi-call systems [18] fuse several outputs—a premise contested by Self-MoA [19]; see [6] for a survey. All of these methods improve upon, or compare against, the per-instance oracle, yet treat that oracle as ground truth. 

Evaluation ceilings and their bias. RouterBench [7], RouterEval [20], and the large-scale LLMRouterBench [8] define and popularize the per-instance oracle and the ∼20 -point gap (“model-recall failure”). These ceilings are built from a single correctness draw per (query, model), and that draw is itself stochastic. LLMRouterBench’s oracle is a per-query union —it deems a query solvable if any model pro-duces a correct prediction in hindsight (Sec. 3.4 of [8]; 

aggregators.py )—over predictions sampled at tempera-ture 0.2 [8], i.e. a union of stochastic single draws. Router-Bench instead defines a best-single-model (argmax over per-model scores) oracle; its decoding parameters are undisclosed, so the single-draw stochasticity is plausible but unverified, and on binary correctness its argmax coincides numerically with a union. In either construction the reported oracle Oexp 

is, under stochastic decoding, an upward-biased estimate of a reproducible solve probability [21], [22], [13], [23], [24], [25], [26]—a special case of the classical winner’s curse for maxima over noisy estimates [11], [27], [28], [29], [30], [31], with Best-of-∞ [10] exhibiting precisely this for test-time ensembling. The reproducible oracle Orepro therefore sits below Oexp , and the difference Gnoise is single-draw label noise rather than recoverable routing headroom. This work carries the statistical observation to the routing-benchmark oracle; the observation itself is not claimed as novel. Two recent works also argue the routing ceiling is in-flated; they differ from this work as follows. The Unsolvabil-ity Ceiling [15] recomputes RouterBench/LLMRouterBench-style oracles, decomposes the gap, and proposes a corrected

This work therefore frames oracle inflation as having two orthogonal sources —deterministic evaluation artifacts (prior work) and stochastic single-draw label noise at T > 0 (this paper)—and contributes the missing axis: a k ≥ 10 multi-sample re-estimation (per-model solve-probability technique following [33]), a reproducible-specialist-vs-single-draw-noise decomposition, and a multi-sample oracle protocol (Table I). A separate line uses multi-sample success probabilities to build 

routers [34], [35] rather than to audit the oracle. 

Concurrent decomposition work. Two contemporaneous works decompose the oracle gap along the same axis and clarify where the contribution of this work lies. Closest is the Capability Frontier [14], which independently observes that the per-instance oracle takes a maximum over noisy sam-ple means—inflating it through single-model and single-run bias—and de-biases it by extrapolating over many generations. This work takes the opposite direction: rather than extrapolate the inflation away, it shows the single-run component is an 

irreducible floor for any single-commit router (Thm. 2(a)) and characterizes a recoverability asymmetry —the floor is un-capturable by selection yet closed by test-time sampling [36], [37], and only up to a verifier-gated ceiling [38], [39]—which the debiasing view does not address. “When Does Combining LMs Help?” [40] asks the same question as this paper but through a deterministic error-correlation (co-failure) ceiling, an axis orthogonal to the stochastic single-draw axis studied here. The contribution of this work is thus the irreducibility framing and the recoverability asymmetry—not the observa-tion that oracles are inflated, which is now shared. III. Sections III–IV establish, as theorems, that the single-draw oracle is upward-biased (Prop. 1), that the router-to-oracle gap splits exactly into a recoverable term and a non-negative single-draw-noise term (Thm. 1), and that this noise is analytically lower-bounded and grows in the hard regime — a noise share S(K) ↑ 1−p of the oracle (Cor. 1) and a Θ(1 − ¯p)

fraction of every router’s gap where no model is reliable (Cor. 2). The experiments below therefore do not argue that the gap is partly noise—that is proven; they only localize 

where existing benchmarks fall on this proven structure, i.e. how large the noise term is on real model pools.

The answer this work reaches is two-sided. Most of the router-to-oracle gap is real, recoverable specialist advantage: 

64 –88% of it is Grec that a better single-commit router can close. But a substantial, previously unquantified minority is not: single-draw label noise is 12% of the gap on a saturated benchmark, 36% on an unsaturated one, and 43% on the hardest, no-model-reliable queries; none of it is recoverable by any router, only by resampling (with a verifier for the guessing residual). Oracle-gap headroom is therefore systematically overstated, and most so exactly where no model is reliable (thin support); the “model-recall failure” diagnosis [8] con-flates a genuine selection deficit with this irreducible single-draw inflation. Two design consequences follow. For benchmark design-ers : reporting a multi-sample oracle—the reproducible ceiling 

Orepro and the expected oracle Oexp with its noise share 

S(K)—would avoid the single-draw union, which our results PREPRINT, JULY 2026 13 

show can attribute a third of the gap (nearly half on hard queries) to unreachable noise. For routing research : correlated pools inflate the apparent gap (eleven models here act like two to three independent ones), and the recoverable part is smaller than reported numbers imply, so progress is more likely to come from better ex-ante quality estimation and from decorrelating the pool than from pursuing an inflated ceiling. The inflation we quantify is orthogonal to the deterministic artifacts of [15]: theirs is greedy-decoding measurement error, ours is stochastic single-draw variance; a faithful oracle must control both. VIII. C ONCLUSION 

This paper revisited the per-instance oracle that anchors LLM-routing benchmarks. It showed that, under stochastic decoding, the single-draw oracle is an upward-biased estimate of any reproducible ceiling, and decomposed the router-to-oracle gap into recoverable specialist advantage and single-draw label noise through cheap multi-sample re-estimation that needs no new router. On a controlled open-pool re-generation, single-draw label noise accounts for a substantial, previously unquantified minority of both the gap and the “model-recall failure” diagnosis—larger on unsaturated benchmarks and approaching half on the hardest, no-model-reliable queries— rather than recoverable routing headroom (Section VI); the majority remains genuine specialist advantage. For benchmark designers, the practical recommendation is to report a multi-sample oracle (expected and reproducible variants) rather than a single-draw one; for routing research, the recoverable head-room is smaller and harder than reported numbers imply, so progress is more likely to come from better quality estimation than from closing an inflated gap. The contributions of this work are the single-draw bias analysis, the gap decomposition, and a released corrected-oracle evaluation protocol. 

Future work. Our estimates use k samples at a single temperature on an open-model pool; extending to larger k,multiple temperatures, and live frontier pools would sharpen the estimate and test whether the bias grows with pool strength. More fundamentally, the finding motivates a program: (i) building cheap, calibrated ex-ante quality estimators and map-ping calibration to realized routing gain; (ii) characterizing how cross-model estimator-error correlation affects routing optimality; and (iii) re-evaluating cost–quality claims once end-to-end latency is priced on live frontier pools—so that routing progress is measured against a calibrated oracle rather than an inflated one.
