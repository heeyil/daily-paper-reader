# Policy Regret for Embedding Model Routing: Contextual Bandits with Low-Rank Experts 

Yan Dai ‚àó Negin Golrezaei ‚ÄÝ Patrick Jaillet ‚Ä°

Abstract 

Modern recommendation systems increasingly rely on dynamically routing diverse queries to multiple embedding models. Despite its practical significance, this problem remains poorly understood under realistic conditions like adversarial queries, bandit feedback, and limited observability of models. We formalize embedding model routing as an adversarial contextual linear bandit with low-rank experts, where contexts are queries, actions are items, and experts are the embedding models working on low-rank latent representation spaces. We first establish that standard regret notions suffer from structural misspecification or statistical intractability, and we identify a log-quadratic policy class that is expressive enough to capture query-dependent model routing, yet structured enough to allow efficient online learning. Second, we propose a policy gradient algorithm called Hypentropy Policy Gradient (HPG). It provably adapts to the unknown low-rank structure under incomplete information and attains eO(s‚àöM T ) linearized policy regret ‚Äì where s, M , and T are the intrinsic rank of the experts, the number of models, and the number of rounds ‚Äì thus avoiding a curse of dimensionality. Finally, we also provide an computationally efficient and parameter-free implementation of HPG. 

# 1 Introduction 

Modern recommendation systems and search engines rely heavily on dense embedding models for information retrieval (Yi et al., 2019; Karpukhin et al., 2020). These models map queries and items into low-dimensional ‚Äúembedding spaces,‚Äù enabling efficient retrieval based on the similarity scores (i.e., inner products). However, representing the full diversity of distinct user queries within a single embedding space is fundamentally limited: Due to models‚Äô intrinsic low-dimensional structures, no single embedding model can be uniformly optimal across all types of queries (Weller et al., 2026). This limitation is reflected in practice. Large-scale industrial e-commerce platforms by Alibaba and Meta deploy multiple embedding models to capture different semantic aspects of queries (Li et al., 2019a; Huang et al., 2020). Similarly, recent approaches in Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) route queries to specialized ‚Äúexpert models‚Äù trained on domain-specific corpora, such as code, finance, or medical data (Lee et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2026). These developments point to a challenge: How to dynamically route each incoming query to the most appropriate embedding model? Towards answering this question in a theoretically grounded way, we make the following main contributions: 

Modeling (Section 2). We frame the online embedding model routing problem as a T -round adversarial contextual linear bandit with low-rank experts: In round t, the learner observes an adversarial query qt ‚àà Rdq as context and faces an item set A ‚äÜ Rda as actions . M embedding models, as experts , each implicitly has a distribution over A via a latent embedding space of dimension 

s ‚â™ min (dq, d a). The learner can only choose one model mt to route, observe the selected model‚Äôs recommendation, and receive an induced noisy reward. The goal is to compete with a fixed class of routing policies. This formulation captures the main challenges in realistic recommendation systems: adversarial query sequences, bandit reward feedback, and partial observability of model outputs. 

Regret Notion (Section 3). A key challenge is identifying an appropriate performance benchmark for this problem. We find standard regret notions inadequate: competing with the best fixed model (i.e., expert regret) ignores query heterogeneity, while competing with arbitrary query-dependent policies (i.e., contextual regret) is intractable. The commonly used log-linear policy class also fails to fit the underlying structures of embedding models. Instead, our main insight is, under the latent geometry of embedding models, the expected reward of each model is approximately a 

low-rank quadratic function of the query (Proposition 1). This leads to our log-quadratic policy class: expressive enough to capture near-optimal routing, while remaining amenable to efficient learning. In Table 2, we numerically justify our claims on an Amazon ESCI dataset (Reddy et al., 2022).

By automatically adapting to the unknown low-rank structure of embedding models, HPG provably attains eO(s‚àöM T ) linearized policy regret, thus avoiding the curse of dimensionality. 

Practical Implementation (Section 5). Our algorithm builds upon Online Mirror Descent (OMD), which is typically computationally expensive due to a Bregman projection. Perhaps surprisingly, we prove that our projection step in HPG can be implemented in O(d2

1.1 Related Literature 

Model Routing. In large-scale industrial recommendation systems or searching engines, multi-embedding frameworks were developed for better performance (Li et al., 2019a; Huang et al., 2020). 2Policy Regret for Embedding Model Routing Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures were proposed in the natural language processing literature to aggregate representations and capture semantic intents (Shazeer et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2018; Lepikhin et al., 2021; Fedus et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022). In the era of large language models (LLMs), dynamic model routing (also known as model cascading or LLM routing) was proposed to balance the generation quality and the inference cost (Jiang et al., 2023; Shnitzer et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Ong et al., 2025). More closely related to our context of embedding models, modern Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines increasingly route queries to specialized expert retrievers (Mallen et al., 2023; Jeong et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2026). Several prior works also drew heuristics by viewing model routing as (online or offline) contextual bandits (Nguyen et al., 2024; Ong et al., 2025; Hu et al., 2025; Tsiourvas et al., 2025; Jitkrittum et al., 2026; Zu et al., 2026; Poon et al., 2026), but they treated the underlying embedding models as complete black-boxes. On the other hand, in this paper, we model the embedding model routing rigorously as an adversarial contextual linear bandit with low-rank experts, and design a policy gradient algorithm with provable regret guarantee, computational cost bound, and parameter-free implementation. 

Bandits with Expert Advice. Bandits with expert advice (Definition 1) were first studied by Auer et al. (2002), who proposed the EXP4 algorithm for O(‚àöKT log M ) expert regret where K, T, M 

are the numbers of arms, rounds, and experts. Beygelzimer et al. (2011) proposed EXP4.P, achieving the same bound with high probability. Seldin et al. (2013) raised the limited observation setup ‚Äì where each round only N experts can be queried ‚Äì as a COLT open problem. With full information, 

eO(pM T /N ) expert regret is possible (Seldin et al., 2014); under bandit feedback, Kale (2014) gave 

eO(pmin( N, K )M T /N ) expert regret and a near-matching lower bound. The unlimited observation setup has recently regained interest, with Œ©( pKT log( M/K ) ) lower bounds established for learners with different levels of adaptivity (Ito, 2024; Cesa-Bianchi et al., 2025; Chase et al., 2025). 

(Contextual) Linear Bandits. Linear bandit was studied by Dani et al. (2008), and the contextual version (Definition 2) was studied by Chu et al. (2011) and Abbasi-Yadkori et al. (2011) under the adversarial-context stochastic-reward (‚ÄúA-S‚Äù) regime. The adversarial-context and adversarial-reward (‚ÄúA-A‚Äù) regime is intractable (Kanade and Steinke, 2014; Hazan and Koren, 2016; Neu and Olkhovskaya, 2020), thus Agarwal et al. (2014) and Syrgkanis et al. (2016) opted for policy regret. The stochastic-context adversarial-reward (‚ÄúS-A‚Äù) regime was proposed by Neu and Olkhovskaya (2020) under the known context distribution assumption. Subsequent works (Neu and Olkhovskaya, 2021; Luo et al., 2021; Dai et al., 2023; Sherman et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2024) focused on the more general linear Markov decision process (MDP) setup; when specialized to bandits, they require extra assumptions or give sub-optimal regret. Liu et al. (2023) proposed an efficient eO(d2‚àöT ) algorithm and an inefficient eO(d‚àöT ) algorithm, with d being the dimension and T being the number of rounds. Ito (2024) considered a finite-arm case and van Erven et al. (2025) refined the computational cost. Misspecification in linear bandits were studied by, for example, Gopalan et al. (2016); Ghosh et al. (2017); Foster et al. (2020); Dong and Yang (2023); Liu et al. (2024), which we adopt in Eqs. (2) and (4). Richer non-linear regimes were also studied in the literature (Russo and Van Roy, 2013; Foster et al., 2018; Foster and Rakhlin, 2020; Li et al., 2022a), which are beyond the scope of this paper. 

Policy Gradient Methods. Policy gradient (PG) methods are foundational for reinforcement learning and control (Williams, 1992; Sutton et al., 1999; Kakade, 2001). Global convergence of PG methods for the log-linear policy class was initiated by Fazel et al. (2018) in control theory and Bhandari and Russo (2024) in reinforcement learning. Agarwal et al. (2021) and Mei et al. (2020) proposed gradient dominance conditions to establish exact convergence rates in tabular settings, 3Policy Regret for Embedding Model Routing but the log-linear policy class remains open (see Appendix B.4). Policy gradient is also frequently designed from an OMD perspective (Geist et al., 2019; Shani et al., 2020), where various entropy regularizers exist (Alfano et al., 2023; Cichocki et al., 2025). However, we are unaware of existing works utilizing hypentropy in policy gradient to promote low-rankness of the learned parameter. 

Parameter-Free Online Learning. Parameter-free online learning attains near-optimal regret without prior information on the comparator (Chaudhuri et al., 2009; Streeter and McMahan, 2012; Orabona, 2013; McMahan and Orabona, 2014). Orabona and P√°l (2016) proposed a ‚Äúcoin-betting‚Äù framework, which enables black-box frameworks for parameter-free online learning in richer regimes (Cutkosky and Orabona, 2018). More generally, adaptivity to, e.g., optimal action‚Äôs loss (Freund and Schapire, 1997; Allenberg et al., 2006; Allen-Zhu et al., 2018), feedback delays (Zimmert and Seldin, 2020; Gyorgy and Joulani, 2021; Huang et al., 2023), or properties of loss distributions (Huang et al., 2022; Genalti et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025), has been widely studied in bandits or online learning.
