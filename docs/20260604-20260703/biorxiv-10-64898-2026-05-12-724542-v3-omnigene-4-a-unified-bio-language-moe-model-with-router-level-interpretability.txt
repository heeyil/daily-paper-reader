Abstract 6

How do multi-modal large language models that jointly process natural language and biological 7

sequences (DNA, protein, structural alphabets) actually answer biological questions, especially 8

sequence-grounded questions whose answer depends on residue-level patterns rather than litera-9

ture recall? We introduce OmniGene-4 , a unified bio-language Mixture-of-Experts foundation 10 

model on Gemma-4-26B-A4B (128 experts/layer, top-8 routing), and use its discrete router 11 

state to dissect this question. By hooking every router across eight task families, we provide the 12 

first router-level decomposition for a biological MoE: continued pretraining (CPT) accounts for 13 

96% of cross-task expert differentiation and supervised fine-tuning (SFT) for 4% , reshaping 14 

middle and output layers respectively. Within the protein-homology task family, per-pair routing 15 

divergence stays below 0.04 (vs 0.23 cross-task), implying that sequence-grounded decisions 16 

occur inside expert computation rather than at the gate — the gate selects the modality, the 17 

experts compute the answer. The pipeline yields strong benchmarks: remote-homology 82.60% 18 

(vs ESM-2 3B, MMseqs2, DIAMOND by 28–31 pp); standard homology 99.40% ; BixBench 19 

(general biological-knowledge) 93.66% . A dual-head architecture adds per-residue 3Di/DSSP 20 

classifiers (78.6%/100%). To probe whether the discovered transfer mechanism is robust un-21 

der modality scaling, we further extend the model to OmniGene-4-MM , adding four vision 22 

modalities (chemical-structure images, medical/pathology imagery, charts) via a vision tower 23 

and a three-stage LoRA pipeline at 1.5 GPU-days total. The multi-modal model preserves the 24 

homology capability ( 85% standard, 69.5% remote) and acquires chemist-readable structure 25 

understanding ( 96% on Vis-CheBI20 functional-group captioning) while consuming roughly 26 

four orders of magnitude less compute than recent specialized MoE bio-models. The work char-27 

acterizes how multi-modal bio-foundation models acquire, route, and preserve sequence-aware 28 

capability — central to the next generation of scientific large language models. 29 

Keywords: bioinformatics, foundation model, mixture-of-experts, interpretability, protein homology, 30 

multi-modal learning, modality-invariant transfer. 31

The bigger picture. 

Modern AI models that read both human language and biological sequences (DNA, proteins) often behave like black boxes: we see their answers but not the inner mechanism that produced them. This matters for biology, where a wrong but confidently worded answer can mislead an experiment that costs months and tens of thousands of dollars. We use Mixture-of-Experts architecture — a transformer where each token is routed to a small subset of 128 specialized sub-networks at every layer — to make this internal mechanism legible. By logging which experts are activated for each input, we show that adding biology-specific pre-training to a general-purpose language model causes the routing to spontaneously partition the network into modality-specific sub-networks, and that the same partitioning re-emerges when we further extend the model to also process molecular images, medical images, and chart images. The same model achieves protein-homology accuracy that surpasses classical sequence-alignment tools (MMseqs2, DIAMOND) and recent protein language models (ESM-2 3B), at roughly four orders of magnitude less compute than recent specialized MoE bio-models. The work is a step toward foundation models for biology that are simultaneously broad in modality coverage, mechanistically transparent, and economically reproducible by groups outside the largest industrial labs.

## 1 Introduction 34 

The central scientific question of this work is: how do multi-modal large language models that jointly 35 

process natural language and biological sequences — DNA, protein, structural alphabets such as 36 

Foldseek 3Di — actually answer biological questions? The question has two parts. The easy part is 37 

literature-style questions whose answer can be retrieved from natural-language pretraining (“what 38 

does the BRCA1 gene do?”). The hard part, and the focus of this paper, is sequence-grounded 39 

questions where the answer depends on residue-level patterns in the input sequence rather than 40 

on prior literature recall — “are these two protein sequences structurally related?”, “what is the 41 

secondary structure of this residue?”, “what is the 3Di letter at position 47?”. For these questions, 42 

the model must do real work on the sequence at inference time; it cannot retrieve the answer from 43 

text memorized at pretraining. How that work is distributed inside the model — which subnetworks 44 

process the protein letters, which contribute to the natural-language framing, where the cross-modal 45 

binding happens — is currently a black box for almost every bio-foundation model. 46 

The field offers many such models. Protein language models — ESM-2 [ 36], its predecessor 47 

ESM-1b [ 48 ], ProtTrans/ProtT5 [ 13 ], ProstT5 [ 24] — and dedicated remote-homology search 48 

tools [ 21, 37, 49 ] have pushed protein remote-homology detection toward what appears to be a 49 

performance ceiling. Genomic foundation models including DNABERT [ 29], DNABERT-2 [ 64], the 50 

Nucleotide Transformer [ 10], HyenaDNA [ 43], and the multimodal Evo/Evo 2 family [ 4 , 44] have 51 

reached trillion-token scale. Single-cell transcriptomics has seen a parallel rise in foundation models 52 

such as scGPT [ 8], scFoundation [ 22 ], and Geneformer [ 56]. General-purpose LLMs fine-tuned on 53 

biological corpora [ 2 , 20 , 38, 61] have joined the race. Cross-modal efforts such as LucaOne [ 23 ]54 

have begun the unification of DNA and protein modalities. But almost all of these models are dense 55 

transformers, where the same MLP weights process AGCTAGCT and “the quick brown fox” , and any 56 

claim about “a DNA submodel inside” must rely on probing classifiers or representation geometry, 57 

neither of which gives a direct answer to “which neurons fired on this input” . Whether and how 58 

multi-modal sequence-aware capability is structurally organized inside these models is therefore 59 

largely unresolved. 60 

We argue that Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures offer the most direct path to answering this 61

and the identities of the selected experts are simply integers that can be logged. The router is a 63 

discrete gate on a continuous feature bundle — it partitions token-level computation explicitly. This 64 

provides an unusually direct substrate to ask: when a multi-modal bio-foundation model answers 65 

a sequence-grounded biological question, which experts at which layers were activated, and how 66 

did training shape that activation pattern? Recent MoE scaling work [ 9 , 11, 60] and MoE-focused 67 

interpretability studies on general LLMs [ 34, 46 ] have shown that this is tractable, but no systematic 68 

router-level analysis has been reported for a biological foundation model. 69 

Within this framing, two sub-questions are central. (i) Where does sequence-aware capa-70 

bility come from? The standard recipe is continued pretraining (CPT) on biological corpora 71 

followed by supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on instruction examples [ 28, 31 , 32], and the literature has 72 

documented a non-trivial balance between the two stages, including the phenomenon of catastrophic 73 

forgetting [ 26, 39]. But which stage installs the cross-modal partitioning of computation, and which 74 

stage merely re-aligns the output? Without a direct measurement, practitioners cannot rationally 75 

allocate compute. (ii) Where does sequence-grounded decision-making happen? If two 76 

protein sequences activate the same set of experts at every layer, then the gate is not where the 77 

homology call is being made — the answer must be computed inside the expert FFNs on residue-level 78 

signal. Conversely, if homologous and non-homologous pairs activate disjoint routing patterns, the 79 

gate itself is doing the work. The existing bio-interpretability program [ 1 , 3, 6, 55 ] has not addressed 80 

this distinction with router-level data. 81 

This paper provides the first quantitative measurements of both sub-questions for a multi-modal 82 

bio-foundation MoE. 83 

Our contribution 84 

This paper makes four contributions. 85 

(1) OmniGene-4, a unified bio-language foundation model on MoE. We build on 86 

Gemma-4-26B-A4B-Instruct [ 19 ] (30 transformer layers, 128 experts per layer, top-8 routing, ≈ 3.8 B87 

active parameters per token). We inject a 28,028-token biological vocabulary (DNA BPE over 32 GB, 88 

protein BPE over 16 GB, 20 Foldseek 3Di letters [ 58], 8 DSSP labels [ 33 ], and control tokens such 89 

as <PRO_M> , <DNA_A> , <3Di_p> , <2D_H> ), extending the embedding table from 262 ,144 to 290 ,172 .90 

Continued pretraining uses QLoRA [ 12, 25 ] ( r=64, α=128, 4-bit NF4 quantization, eight target 91 

modules including router.proj ) on 32.5 GB of mixed DNA + protein + OpenWebText + 3Di 92 

+ DSSP data for 0.6 epoch. Supervised fine-tuning is performed in four cumulative passes (v2 93 

through v5): Bio-SFT v2 on 179K examples covering eight task families; v3 adds 20K remote-94 

homology pairs; v4 introduces a pure-Alpaca prompt template, instruction-tuning loss masking, and 95 

Structure/Mutation oversampling; v5 adds two per-residue classification heads (3Di and DSSP) 96 

trained jointly with generation under a 0.5/0.5 loss split. Each stage initializes from the previous 97 

LoRA + embedding, so v5 weights cumulatively reflect all prior training. 98 

(2) A benchmark suite spanning sequence-grounded and general biological QA. 99 

We evaluate three protocols that together cover the spectrum from pure sequence reasoning to 100 

general biological knowledge. (2a) Standard protein homology on BioPAWS [ 61] (99.40%) tests 101 

close-homolog detection, where surface sequence similarity is informative. (2b) Remote homology 102 

on the same dataset ( 82.60% ) tests structure-level recognition under sequence divergence and is 103 

the hardest sequence-grounded task: on the identical 500-pair set, ESM-2 [ 36] (both 650M and 3B), 104 

MMseqs2 [ 52 ], and DIAMOND [ 5] all saturate at 50–55%; our model exceeds them by 28–31 pp, and 105 

scaling ESM-2 from 650M to 3B adds only +0 .7 pp, ruling out encoder capacity as the bottleneck. 106 

(2c) BixBench [17 ] ( 93.66% ) is the most general benchmark in this suite: a true/false test 107

Implications 145 

We summarize the picture as a tentative representation/output-alignment factorization of bio-146 

foundation training: under the layer-averaged JS metric, CPT does most of the work of repartitioning 147 

routing across modalities (and does so in the middle transformer layers), while SFT mostly adjusts 148 

the routing nearest lm_head to align output format with task labels. We are deliberately cautious 149 

here. The factorization is descriptive of one architecture and one training run; the routing data 150 

themselves were collected at v3 (baseline, CPT-only, and CPT+v3-SFT), so the SFT contribution 151 

measured here is the contribution of v2+v3 instruction-tuning specifically, not the additional SFT-152 

recipe changes introduced by v4 (Alpaca template, loss masking) or v5 (dual-head classifiers). The 153

2.1 Training 166 

The CPT phase converged smoothly. Loss decreased monotonically over 1,680 gradient steps (0.6 167 

epoch) with no observed instability, ending at ≈ 36% of starting loss. Validation loss on a held-out 168 

0.5% slice of the binary corpus tracked training loss within ±2% , indicating no overfitting at this 169

2.5 Multi-modal extension: OmniGene-4-MM and modality-invariant transfer 384 

The router-level analysis in §2.3 establishes that OmniGene-4 partitions modality processing across 385 

distinct experts. It does not establish whether the partitioning generalizes when new modalities 386 

are introduced post-hoc, or whether the syntactic-isomorphism transfer that makes paraphrase 387 

fine-tuning succeed on protein homology is preserved when the model is asked to do something 388 

else simultaneously. We address both questions by extending v5 into a multi-modal generalist 389 

(OmniGene-4-MM ) covering vision, sequence, and language inputs. 390 

2.5.1 Architecture and training pipeline 391 

Starting from the v5 merged checkpoint, we attach the Gemma-4 vision encoder [ 19] (27 transformer 392 

layers, 1152 hidden dimensions, patch size 16, 2520 visual patches per image) and inject a fresh 393 

LoRA adapter ( r=64 , α=128 , dropout 0.05 ) on the same eight modules used in CPT — Q/K/V/O, 394 

gate/up/down, and router.proj . Figure 7 summarizes the architecture. 395 

Training proceeds in three stages on a single H20 GPU. 396 

Stage 1: vision warmup ( ∼ 0.4 GPU-days). Images-only SFT on a 100K-example pool drawn 397 

from Vis-CheBI20 [ 14] (chemical structures), PubMedVision [ 7 ] and HPA10M [ 27] (medical and 398 

pathology), ChartQA [ 40] (charts), and BiomedVis (synthetic biomedical visual tasks). Learning 399 

rate 5×10 −5, ∼ 10 K steps. This stage activates the vision tower but, by construction, induces 400 

catastrophic forgetting on text: only 4 of 200 BioPAWS prompts return a parseable response. 401 

Stage 2: mixed text + vision ( ∼ 1 GPU-day). A 60:40 mixture of (i) the v5 SFT corpus 402 

(199K text examples) and (ii) the Stage 1 vision pool, 6,000 steps at LR 5×10 −6. This recovers text 403 

capability ( 200 /200 valid responses on the BioPAWS prompts) while preserving vision performance 404 

(≥ 88% on Vis-CheBI20 functional-group recognition). 405

Representation vs alignment: a training-stage factorization. The observation that, under 490 

the layer-averaged JS metric, the CPT stage accounts for the bulk of cross-task routing differentiation 491 

while the SFT stage adds little, is to our knowledge the first such measurement reported for a 492 

biological MoE. It complements recent empirical work on CPT/SFT balance in general LLMs 493 

[ 28 , 32 ] and on fine-grained expert specialization [ 9, 34], extending these findings into the biological 494 

domain. We frame the observation as a tentative hypothesis rather than an established finding: the 495 

two stages of post-pretraining appear to do qualitatively different things, with CPT producing the 496 

bulk of routing change in middle transformer layers ( L11 –L22 ) and SFT producing concentrated 497 

routing change at the layers nearest lm_head (L28 –L29 ). The natural reading is “CPT mostly does 498 

representation differentiation, SFT mostly does output alignment”. We are explicit that this is 499 

exploratory: a single architecture, a single seed, no prompt-bootstrap variance, and an SFT stage 500 

that bundles instruction-tuning with the additional 20K remote pairs. 501 

If the hypothesis holds up under replication, it has practical consequences. When a practitioner 502 

observes a disappointing remote-homology result, the conventional response is to add more labeled 503 

examples for that task. Our measurements suggest this response may be misdirected: the router 504 

appears to have already partitioned proteins into a shared expert pool during CPT, and additional 505 

remote pairs at SFT shift mixture weights only marginally. The corresponding prediction is that 506 

richer and more distant-protein-aware CPT corpora should be more effective per unit of compute 507 

than scaling up SFT. 508

Catastrophic forgetting is avoidable, but the mechanism matters. In our earlier v1 509 

experiments with LLaMA-3.1 8B + Bio-SFT (no instruction replay), BixBench dropped by 17 510 

points (80% → 63%), matching the forgetting pattern documented in [ 26 , 39]. In the present v3, 511 

with 20% OpenWebText [ 18] in the CPT mixture and ∼ 20% instruction replay in the SFT pool, 512 

BixBench rises by 6.7 points relative to Gemma-4-Instruct. The router-level analysis explains why. 513 

Natural-language tokens in v3 are routed to a different set of experts after CPT (NL entropy rises 514 

from 3.85 to 4.13 nats — the router uses more experts for NL, not fewer), while biological tokens 515 

concentrate on a narrower set (Protein entropy falls from 2.76 to 2.54 nats). The two populations 516 

essentially occupy disjoint corners of expert-space. Catastrophic forgetting in dense models can 517 

be thought of as the new training signal overwriting the old; in MoE it instead manifests as one 518 

population displacing another from a shared expert pool. By ensuring the NL population does not 519 

vacate its home experts, we prevent forgetting without paying a capacity cost on the biological side. 520 

Interpretability dividends of MoE. The layer-12 experts described in §2.3.1 (sharply NL-521 

preferring E54, DNA-2-mer-preferring E59/E117, amino-acid-preferring E78, cell-vocabulary E97) 522 

are individually modest findings: most have purities in the 15–46% range, and only one reaches 523 

a clean 80%. We make a corresponding claim of moderate strength. Their collective behaviour 524 

does, however, support a methodological observation that has been articulated for general LLMs 525 

[34 ] but rarely demonstrated for bio-foundation models: MoE routing offers a discrete, auditable 526 

observable for which subnetwork processes which input , in contrast to the continuous probes used in 527 

sparse-autoencoder approaches [ 3 , 55] or attention-based interpretability [ 1 ]. No classifier is fit on 528 

hidden states; the integers logged at the router are causally upstream of the FFN computation. 529 

The broader implication: for safety-sensitive applications (drug interaction prediction, variant 530 

interpretation, clinical decision support), MoE bio-foundation models admit a form of audit that 531 

dense bio-foundation models do not. If the model classifies a novel protein variant as “pathogenic”, 532 

one can check which experts it used and on which residues those experts fired . We did not pursue 533 

this line of inquiry here, but the infrastructure developed in §4.6 supports it directly. 534 

Relation to other bio-foundation models. Our 99.95% standard-homology and 59.50% 535 

remote-homology accuracies place OmniGene-4 in competitive territory on its own evaluation slice .536 

Specialist methods — the classical sequence-search baselines MMseqs2 [ 52 ] and DIAMOND [ 5 ], 537 

embedding-based approaches such as TM-Vec/PLMSearch [ 37], ESM-2 with structural-alignment 538 

heads [ 21], and CATH-based homologous-superfamily detection [ 42] — report 65–75% remote-539 

homology accuracy on differently constructed splits. We did not re-run these methods on the exact 540 

2,000-pair subset used here, and we recognize this comparison as the most important external 541 

benchmarking gap in the present manuscript. Standard protein-evaluation suites such as TAPE [ 47 ]542 

and ProteinGym [ 45], and unified protein-text efforts such as ProtST [ 63], would also be natural 543 

targets for follow-up work. Unified DNA/protein foundation models such as LucaOne [ 23] and 544 

genome-scale models such as Evo [ 4, 44] pursue different design tradeoffs, focusing on sequence 545 

generation rather than instruction-following. The present paper’s contribution is therefore less about 546 

the benchmark ceiling and more about the methodological substrate: a quantitatively reproducible 547 

router-level decomposition of what CPT and SFT each contribute, which may generalize to other 548 

MoE bio-foundation efforts. 549

4.7 Multi-modal extension (OmniGene-4-MM) 721 

The multi-modal extension reported in §2.5 adds the Gemma-4 vision encoder to v5 and trains a 722 

fresh LoRA adapter through three SFT stages. 723 

Vision tower. We use the native Gemma-4 vision encoder [ 19] unchanged: 27 transformer layers, 724 

hidden size 1152 , patch size 16 , producing 2520 visual patches per image. Patches are projected 725 

into the language-model token embedding space via the model’s built-in multimodal projector, so 726 

visual and text tokens enter the MoE backbone as a unified sequence. 727 

LoRA configuration. A new LoRA adapter (denoted stage2 in the code, retained as the adapter 728 

key in subsequent stages for state-dict compatibility) is injected into the Gemma-4 language-model 729 

backbone with r= 64 , α=128 , dropout 0.05 , on the eight target modules used in CPT ( q_proj ,730 

k_proj , v_proj , o_proj , gate_proj , up_proj , down_proj , router.proj ). The vision encoder is 731 

fully frozen. 732 

Training data. Stage 1 uses an 100K-example image-only mixture: Vis-CheBI20 [ 14 ] train 733 

split (Vis-CheBI20 train, ∼ 11 K), PubMedVision [ 7] ( ∼ 30 K), HPA10M [ 27] ( ∼ 25 K microscopy), 734 

ChartQA [ 40] ( ∼ 10 K), and BiomedVis synthetic visual tasks ( ∼ 24 K). Stage 2 mixes Stage 1 vision 735 

data ( 30 K) with the v5 SFT corpus ( 30 K text). Stage 3 v3 mixes 45 K text examples (with the 736 

homology subset from BioPAWS oversampled 3×) and 5K vision replay. 737 

Stage hyperparameters. 738 

• Stage 1: learning rate 5×10 −5 with cosine schedule and 3% warmup, ∼ 10 K steps, batch size 1 739 

with gradient accumulation 32 (effective batch 32), max sequence length 1536, paged-AdamW-740 

8bit optimizer, gradient checkpointing. 741 

• Stage 2: continues from Stage 1 LoRA + embedding; learning rate 5 × 10 −6, 6,000 steps, 742 

otherwise identical. 743 

• Stage 3 v2 (failure mode reported in Table 7): learning rate 5×10 −6, 6,000 steps, embedding 744 

trainable. Plateaued at Stage 2 levels and was abandoned. 745 

• Stage 3 v3 (final): learning rate 2×10 −5 (4× v2), 3,000 steps, embedding frozen , 3× homology 746
