Abstract 1

AI-scientist systems are beginning to automate parts of scientific research. We present C ONTINUUM CELL AGENT ,2

an autonomous agent that executes literature review, hypothesis formation, computational experimentation, manuscript 3

drafting, and adversarial peer review as a single unattended run. Existing AI scientist systems remain difficult to 4

diagnose because they lack modularity, systematic prompt grounding, and observability into long-running behavior. 5

CONTINUUM CELL AGENT addresses these gaps with a modular supernode architecture for stage-wise backend 6

swapping, protocols grounded in curated research-method checklists that also define reviewer rubrics, and a diagnostics 7

layer that records file-based artifacts, message traces, and state transitions. We evaluate the system on open-domain 8

QA benchmarks and biomedical/longevity case studies, showing that it can produce checkable research artifacts while 9

exposing pipeline dynamics for rigorous AI co-scientist research. 10 

# 1 Introduction 11 

The vision of an AI system that autonomously conducts scientific research— formulating hypotheses, designing 12 

experiments, executing code, and writing papers—has motivated a growing body of work [ 3, 5, 13 , 19 ]. Recent 13 

advances in large language models have made this vision increasingly tangible: agents can now search literature, 14 

write and debug code, and produce coherent manuscripts. Yet the path from demonstration to rigorous, reproducible 15 

autonomous science remains fraught with under-explored challenges. 16 

Existing systems typically automate only one or two stages of the research lifecycle. ResearchAgent [3] generates 17 

ideas via knowledge-graph traversal and LLM refinement but does not execute experiments or produce manuscripts. 18 

MLAgentBench [17] benchmarks coding agents on machine-learning tasks but lacks literature review, hypothesis 19 

generation, and paper-writing stages. The AI Scientist [19 ] covers the full cycle— ideation, coding, writing, and 20 

self-review—but operates exclusively on ML-template experiments with pre-written scaffolding, uses a monolithic 21 

architecture that cannot swap agent backends, and provides no diagnostics into where or why the pipeline fails. 22 

We identify three gaps that limit progress toward robust autonomous scientific discovery: 23 

G1. Modular agent composition. No existing system lets researchers plug different agent backends into different 24 

stages of the same pipeline. This makes it impossible to perform controlled ablations—e.g., “Does a planner-25 

executor outperform a simple ReAct agent at modeling when ideation and paper-writing are held constant?”—or 26 

to leverage provider-specific strengths (one model/agent/prompt may excel at retrieval, another at code generation). 27 

The lack of modularity also impedes extensibility: adding a new agent architecture requires rewriting the entire 28 

system rather than swapping a single component. As the landscape of LLM agents evolves rapidly, this rigidity 29 

becomes a significant barrier to systematic research. 30 

G2. Systematic prompt grounding. Agent prompts are typically written ad hoc, reflecting the intuitions of individual 31 

developers rather than established methodological principles. Yet decades of scholarship have codified best 32 

practices for scientific reasoning (Pearl’s causal hierarchy [ 21 ], Popper’s falsifiability [ 22], Chamberlin’s multiple 33 

working hypotheses [ 7]), statistical rigor (ASA Statement on p-values [ 26 ], Gelman’s forking-paths analysis [ 8]), 34 

and scientific writing (Schimel’s OCAR structure [24], Toulmin’s argument model [25]). 35

We use these frameworks to make agent behavior more disciplined and—crucially—to provide the adversarial 36 

reviewer with concrete, auditable criteria. When instructions and evaluation rubrics derive from the same source, 37 

we eliminate the “misalignment tax” where agents optimize for implicit criteria different from those used to judge 38 

them. The prompts can be studied through benchmark results, but they are also part of the explanation for why 39 

an agent reported a claim, rejected a result, or revised a manuscript. Prior systems such as FARS [ 1 ] and The AI 40 

Scientist [ 19 ] discuss paper acceptance or final quality as evaluation signals; we argue that the process of making 41 

the science, including the quality of intermediate reasoning, should also be inspectable. 42 

G3. State-level observability and multi-phase evaluation. Prior work reports final output quality (e.g., paper 43 

acceptance rates, benchmark scores) but not where in the pipeline the agent struggles or how revision loops affect 44 

quality over time. As autonomous research systems become longer-running and more complex, final-outcome 45 

metrics alone are insufficient. We need fine-grained observability: Which stage consumes the most time? Where 46 

do failures cluster? Do additional revision iterations improve quality, or do they risk context-window exhaustion? 47 

Furthermore, evaluation itself is multifaceted: automated benchmarks with known answers test correctness; AI 48 

reviewers provide scalable quality estimation; and expert case studies validate real-world utility. No prior system 49 

integrates all three evaluation modalities. 50 

CONTINUUM CELL AGENT addresses all three gaps. Its contributions are: 51 

C1. Modular Supernode Architecture (Section 3). We organize the research lifecycle as a LangGraph state machine 52 

with three supernodes —Ideation, Modeling, and Paper—each defined by a protocol, a tool set, message memory, 53 

file artifacts, and an iteration budget. Each supernode is followed by an adversarial review gate that can accept 54 

(advancing to the next stage) or reject and route back to any upstream stage. Crucially, the agent backend 55 

powering each supernode is a configuration choice: command-line flags such as --modeler react and --modeler 56 

planner-executor swap the Modeling backend, enabling controlled experiments. This modularity also facilitates 57 

agent-level hyperparameter optimization —an under-explored research direction where the “hyperparameters” are 58 

architectural choices like agent type, iteration budget, and tool set. 59 

C2. Framework-Grounded Protocols (Section 3.2). We compiled operationalizable methodological frameworks 60 

into a reference document and encoded the relevant subset as stage-specific checklists embedded directly in 61 

agent protocols. For Ideation: Chamberlin’s multiple hypotheses, Popper’s falsifiability, Pearl’s causal ladder, 62 

Heilmeier’s catechism. For Modeling: Tukey’s EDA protocol, Gelman’s forking-paths documentation, ASA’s 63 

statistical rigor. For Paper: Schimel’s OCAR narrative, Toulmin’s argument structure, Feynman’s integrity principle. 64 

The same checklists serve as evaluation rubrics for the adversarial reviewer, ensuring alignment between agent 65 

instructions and assessment criteria. Every stage ends with a Gawande-style DO-CONFIRM exit checklist that 66 

catches systematic omissions before yielding control. 67 

C3. State Dynamics Diagnostics & Multi-Phase Benchmarking (Section 3.3, Section 4). A built-in diagnostics 68 

layer emits structured timeline events ( stage_start , stage_end ) to a JSONL file, recording per-stage wall-clock 69 

time, iteration counts, and outcomes. Analysis scripts produce stage-level profiling, Gantt-style timelines, and 70 

failure-mode taxonomies. We evaluate C ONTINUUM CELL AGENT with biomedical/longevity case studies and 71 

open-domain QA benchmarks, and use the traces to study stage timing, rollback depth, and failure modes. 72

# 3 Method 100 

CONTINUUM CELL AGENT is designed as practical research software rather than a single monolithic prompt. Its main 101 

structure is a LangGraph state machine with three major tasks—Ideation, Modeling, and Paper—implemented as 102 

supernodes . Each supernode owns a stage-specific protocol, tool set, working memory, and file-based artifacts, and 103 

each is followed by an adversarial review gate that can accept the result or route execution back to any previous stage 104 

(Figure 1). This design makes the pipeline useful both as an autonomous research assistant and as an experimental 105 

harness: the same task can be rerun with different agents, models, budgets, or tools while preserving the protocol and 106 

trace format. 107

3.1 Supernodes and Review Gates 108 

Each supernode is defined by a protocol , not by a particular LLM provider. The protocol specifies the stage objective, 109 

allowed tools, memory interface, required output files, iteration budget, and exit checklist. Ideation searches literature 110 

and writes a proposal with competing hypotheses; Modeling writes and executes Python/R/Julia code in a Docker 111 

sandbox and reports statistical results; Paper reads all accumulated artifacts and drafts a manuscript with citations. Full 112 

per-stage tool specifications, budgets, session isolation, and the LLM fallback chain are in Appendix A. 113

File tools read and write durable artifacts such as 114 

research_proposal.md , source code, plots, diagnosis_report.md , manuscripts, and bibliographies. The message 115 

state carries the current task, prior reviewer decisions, summaries, and routing context across the LangGraph. This 116 

separation is important in practice: files make scientific outputs inspectable and rerunnable, while memory lets the 117 

agent coordinate long-horizon decisions without re-parsing the whole workspace at every step. 118 

The review gates are lightweight, tool-free LLM calls that fire after every supernode. Each gate scores the stage’s 119 

output on stage-appropriate criteria—gap validity for ideation, statistical rigor for modeling, novelty and soundness 120 

for the paper—and returns either ACCEPT or a routing directive to an upstream stage. Because the gates use the same 121 

framework-grounded rubrics embedded in the supernode protocols (Section 3.2), agent instructions and evaluation 122 

criteria stay aligned. 123 

Swappable agents and adapters. Because a supernode exposes a stable protocol—inputs, tools, required artifacts, 124 

review rubric, and trace schema—the implementation can swap the backend agent without changing the rest of the 125 

pipeline. A command-line flag (e.g., --modeler react or --modeler planner-executor ) can replace the Modeling 126 

backend while holding ideation, paper writing, tools, and review budget fixed. The same adapter pattern generalizes to 127 

other stages and enables heterogeneous configurations, prompt-adjustment variants, and agent-level hyperparameter 128 

optimization over choices such as backend, prompt variant, iteration budget, and tool set. 129 

Prompt-based evolution. Plan-Evolve is a prompt-adjustment variant of Plan-Execute. It starts from the same initial 130 

stage prompt, runs the agent with the available tools, and then lets the agent revise the prompt text as the run proceeds. 131 

Subsequent attempts therefore use adjusted prompts rather than a separate training procedure. The adapter interface 132 

remains the same for comparing ReAct, Plan-Execute, and Plan-Evolve. 133 

3.2 Framework Grounding as Protocol Design 134 

The protocol for each supernode is grounded in methodological checklists rather than ad hoc instructions. We compiled 135 

operationalizable frameworks into a reference document ( FRAMEWORKS_REFERENCE.md , 1705 lines) and encoded the 136 

relevant subset as stage-specific checklists embedded directly in agent protocols. 137 

The right column of Figure 1 summarizes the mapping from frameworks to pipeline stages and shows that the same 138 

checklists drive both agent execution and reviewer evaluation. 139 

Dual-use design. A checklist serves two roles simultaneously: (1) it guides the agent during execution (“have I 140 

classified my hypothesis on Pearl’s causal ladder?”), and (2) it provides the reviewer with concrete criteria to audit 141 

(“did the paper overclaim causation from Rung-1 evidence?”). Because the review gate fires after every supernode, each 142 

stage’s output is validated before downstream stages consume it. This eliminates the common failure mode where agent 143 

and reviewer operate under different implicit standards, and catches errors early (e.g., a flawed hypothesis is rejected 144 

before expensive modeling begins). 145 

DO-CONFIRM exit protocol. Inspired by Gawande’s Checklist Manifesto , every supernode works freely during its 146 

iteration budget, then runs a mandatory exit checklist before yielding control. This catches systematic omissions (e.g., 147 

missing effect sizes, unreported negative results) without constraining the agent’s exploratory freedom during main 148 

execution. 149

This traceability is what makes the experiments in this paper possible. The saved sources and executed code make 160 

case-study claims checkable, while the state trace supports analyses of time-to-transition, rollback depth, score-versus-161 

cost tradeoffs, and where the system fails or improves under revision. We view these analyses as an opening for future 162 

agent-dynamics work rather than a solved evaluation protocol. 163 

Stage profiling. The timing trace is an auditing device rather than a deterministic claim about how long each stage 164 

must take. It lets us inspect where a particular controlled run spent time, provided the run configuration is kept visible: 165 

per-stage step budgets, maximum revision count, selected run phases, backend latency, tool runtime, and data availability 166 

all affect the observed allocation. In the reported case studies, modeling is the largest wall-clock component, but we 167 

treat that as a descriptive property of these runs under fixed controls, not as an intrinsic law of the workflow. 168 

Failure-mode taxonomy. We observe three primary failure modes: (1) context-window exhaustion after extended 169 

revision loops (the accumulated conversation exceeds provider limits), (2) sandbox execution errors (missing packages, 170 

data download failures), and (3) reviewer hallucination (the reviewer requests changes already present in the manuscript). 171 

Mode (1) is the most common and suggests a need for context summarization between iterations. 172 

Revision productivity. Not all reviewer-triggered revisions are productive. Our diagnostics let us inspect how 173 

reviewer scores and failure risk change as the run approaches its configured revision cap. In the current runs, early 174 

revision cycles tend to improve the manuscript, while later cycles show diminishing returns and greater context-window 175 

risk; this motivates treating max revisions and enabled run phases as experimental controls rather than assuming an 176 

open-ended revision process is always better. 177

# 5 Discussion 254 

The experiments support a narrow claim: protocol-grounded tool loops can produce inspectable biomedical artifacts and 255 

improve a Qwen3.5-27B baseline on TriviaQA, HotpotQA, and 2WikiMultihopQA (Figure 3). They do not establish 256 

autonomous scientific validity. The AD and PDAC cases show why: a run can finish while using synthetic data or 257 

leaving core analysis errors unresolved. The main value is therefore auditability—linking final outputs to sources, code, 258 

state transitions, and reviewer decisions. Timing and rollback traces are instrumentation under a fixed run configuration, 259 

not general metrics. Backend swapping and Plan-Evolve are useful experimental variables only with matched prompts, 260 

tools, and revision budgets. 261 

# 6 Conclusion 262 

We presented C ONTINUUM CELL AGENT , a traceable testbed for protocol-grounded scientific agents. In the current 263 

experiments, it saves code, sources, intermediate states, and reviewer decisions; it also improves a no-tool Qwen3.5-27B 264 

baseline on TriviaQA, HotpotQA, and 2WikiMultihopQA (Figure 3). The caution is equally important: workflow 265 

completion is auditable, but it is not scientific validity. Reliable autonomous science will require stronger provenance 266 

checks, paired backend ablations, and domain-expert review of the biological claims. 267 

# Limitations 268 

The current system remains limited by coupled failure modes common to long-horizon scientific agents: context-window 269 

exhaustion, unstable memory, brittle biomedical data acquisition, mismatches between cited and processed datasets, 270 

shallow recovery from execution errors, and occasional substitution of synthetic or prototype data when real data access 271 

fails. Benchmark results are likewise conditional on task structure, coverage, metric choice, and judging protocol; the 272 

Plan-Evolve rows are also conditional on the prompt-adjustment trajectory used in the run, and this version reports 273 

TriviaQA, HotpotQA, and 2WikiMultihopQA rather than the broader LongBench suite. Finally, automated reviewers 274 

are useful for scalable triage but cannot replace domain-expert review for scientific validity, especially when data 275 

provenance or biological interpretation is at stake. 276

# A Implementation Details 375 

Model serving and providers. Tier-2 QA runs serve Qwen3.5-27B locally through sglang ; the same served model 376 

is used for the no-tool baseline and all agent loops. Tier-1 case-study runs call provider LLMs through the same adapter 377 

layer: the reported Plan-Execute trajectories include Gemini and GPT-family configurations. 378 

Per-stage tool specifications. Each supernode binds a small, stage-specific tool set. Ideation (20-step budget) 379 

can search, fetch, read, and write, and produces references/research_proposal.md . Modeling (30-step budget) 380 

adds execute_code and list_files , producing code/results/diagnosis_report.md . Paper (15-step budget) uses 381 

read/list/write tools over prior artifacts and produces paper/manuscript.md .382 

Session isolation. Every run creates an ephemeral workspace under instances/<session>/ . All file I/O tools 383 

resolve paths relative to this workspace and reject path-traversal attempts. Code executes in a Docker container 384 

(continuum-sandbox ) with NVIDIA CUDA 12.4, pre-installed Python 3.10 packages (PyTorch, scikit-learn, Scanpy, 385 

NumPy, SciPy, pandas, statsmodels, Matplotlib, and Seaborn), R 4.3 packages (DESeq2, Seurat, and clusterProfiler), 386 

and Julia 1.11. Only the session folder is mounted; the host is never exposed. 387 

LLM fallback chain. CONTINUUM CELL AGENT constructs a LangChain .with_fallbacks() chain from available 388 

API keys: Gemini → OpenAI GPT-4.1 → Groq . If the primary provider returns a 429 or 5xx, the request transparently 389 

retries on the next provider, supporting unattended operation without making availability deterministic. 390 

# B Cross-Domain Benchmark Suite (Tier 2) 391 

To evaluate behavior beyond biomedical case studies, Tier 2 focuses on retrieval-QA tasks that stress retrieval fidelity, 392 

multi-hop reasoning, answer extraction, and cross-domain transfer. 393 

Benchmarks evaluated. This version reports the three LongBench v1 English retrieval-QA subsets used in the main 394 

figure: TriviaQA , HotpotQA , and 2WikiMultihopQA . These probe single-hop retrieval, multi-hop reasoning, and 395 

answer faithfulness under a common LongBench scoring protocol. The harness can also cap other text-QA benchmarks 396 

such as SealQA, FRAMES, and BioASQ for future sweeps, but they are not part of the main reported figure. 397 

Sampling. Each benchmark records the exact evaluated tasks. The three selected LongBench subsets use 200-question 398 

samples for ReAct and Plan-Execute and 120-question Plan-Evolve runs with prompt adjustment enabled. For cost-399 

limited sweeps the harness caps a benchmark to the first N questions in dataset order —a deterministic, reproducible 400 

prefix, not a random independent sample. The selected tasks are persisted with the run metadata; comparisons are 401 

treated as paired only when the same examples are available for the strategies being compared. 402 

Scoring. Every answer is scored under multiple metrics so that verbose research-style outputs are compared fairly 403 

against short-answer leaderboards: a lenient substring match, recall of any gold alias, and three LongBench-style 404 

token-F1 variants over progressively tighter answer-span extractions (first sentence, first bold span, and the noun-405 

phrase-tightened bold span used as the fair single-span estimate). We compare against published LongBench anchors at 406 

matched parameter scale where available. 407

Tier-1 biomedical case studies. Each evaluation covers the methodological strengths, integrity concerns, and biological 415

C.1 PDAC (GPT-4 Plan-and-Execute) 417 

Strengths. The agent completes a full analytical pipeline on real large-scale public data, downloading and processing 418 

GSE16515 ( N =52 ) and GSE15471 ( N =78 ) and producing verifiable differential expression outputs with an intact SHA-419 

256 provenance chain. The reproducibility signal is statistically compelling: a cross-cohort directional concordance 420 

of 0.78 against a null expectation of 0.50, corroborated by 100% directional agreement with an external 300-gene 421 

reference set ( p = 8 ×10 −179 ), constitutes publishable replication evidence for a genuine research gap supported 422 

by prior literature. The agent also maintains exemplary transparency by proactively disclosing the substitution of a 423 

microarray workflow for the planned RNA-seq pipeline, clearly reporting the complete failure of enrichment analysis 424 

due to missing dependencies, and consistently distinguishing planned from executed steps. 425 

Weaknesses. The phrase robust replication in the abstract overstates the strength of the findings; it should be revised 426 

to directional agreement under a single analytical framework . Biologically, the study yields no new discoveries: 427 

pathway enrichment failed entirely with no investigation into the cause, foreclosing mechanistic interpretation and 428 

leaving the core research objective unmet. The top-ranked differentially expressed genes are dominated by stromal 429 

markers, and stromal contamination is uncorrected, precluding any inference about tumor cell-intrinsic transcriptional 430 

changes. In sum, the manuscript is methodologically candid but scientifically incomplete, offering a demonstration of 431 

honest legacy microarray analysis rather than an independent scientific contribution. 432 

C.2 AD (Gemini Plan-and-Execute) 433 

Strengths. The agent demonstrates a meaningful degree of methodological self-correction: Phase 2 appropriately 434 

rejects the causal claims regarding ARX made in Phase 1, as the AUPRC of 0.644 fell below the predefined threshold 435 

of 0.85, and a sensitivity analysis was conducted across 12 parameter combinations. The biological gap motivating this 436 

work is genuine: APOE4-associated microglial dysfunction as an early driver of AD represents an active area of inquiry, 437 

and integrating causal inference tools such as SMR and COLOC with single-cell data constitutes a real methodological 438 

gap. The Phase 2 manuscript is more transparent in its framing, explicitly labeling the model as a spatial autoregressive 439 

co-expression model rather than a causal inference system. 440 

Weaknesses. The most critical issue is circular validation: the genes reported as causal drivers of AD were pre-441 

specified in the data-generating process, so the analysis recovers its own input parameters rather than producing genuine 442 

biological discoveries. Additionally, the manuscript claims in Section 3.3 to have analyzed real ROSMAP and MSBB 443 

data, whereas the workspace contains only synthetic files of the form rosmap_synthetic_*.csv with no evidence of 444 

real data acquisition or processing. A second variant of the AD run further portrays a known method—incorporating 445 

aneuploidy score as a covariate in an NB-GLM design—as a novel framework, when this is standard practice in 446 

DESeq2 multivariate design and does not constitute a methodological innovation. As a consequence, the core biological 447 

conclusions are not credible under the available evidence. 448
