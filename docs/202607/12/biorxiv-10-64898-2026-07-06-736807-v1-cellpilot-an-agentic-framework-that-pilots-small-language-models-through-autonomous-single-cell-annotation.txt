# CellPilot: an agentic framework that pilots small language models through autonomous single-cell annotation 

Siqi Jiang 1†, Cong Qi 1†, Yeqing Chen 1, Xun Song 1, Zhi Wei 1* 

> 1

Abstract 

Large language models can annotate cell types from marker gene lists, but they typically operate after preprocessing and clustering are complete, treating annotation as a terminal labeling step rather than controlling the analytical decisions that produce the evidence for cell identity. We present CellPilot, an agentic framework that guides a locally deployable small language model through the full single-cell analysis workflow, from raw count matrices to cluster-level annota-tion. CellPilot combines standard single-cell analysis tools with structured workflow control and observation-guided reasoning, allowing the model to plan analyses, execute tools, inspect inter-mediate results and revise decisions within a traceable session. On GTEx, structured workflow orchestration raised the same 8B model from 0.39 in a prompt-only setting to 0.89, closing most of the gap to GPT-4o (0.92) within the same framework; the framework gain was substantially larger for the smaller backbone across datasets (+0.35 versus +0.19). Across GTEx, Tabula Sapi-ens, and Mouse Cell Atlas, CellPilot achieves cluster-level annotation accuracies of 0.891, 0.750, and 0.773, outperforming representative reference-based, marker-based, and LLM-based methods. CellPilot confidence scores were associated with annotation correctness and supported post hoc filtering, while complete execution traces were retained for each analysis. These results suggest that structured workflow orchestration can be a critical determinant of performance in multi-step single-cell analysis, enabling locally deployable small language models to approach larger proprietary models while preserving transparency and practical usability. 

Keywords: Single-cell RNA sequencing, structured workflow orchestration, small language models, cell type annotation, autonomous analysis 

## Introduction 1

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has transformed the study of cellular heterogeneity by 2

enabling transcriptome-scale profiling of individual cells across tissues, developmental stages and dis-3

ease contexts [1–3], motivating large-scale efforts to construct comprehensive cellular atlases of human 4

tissues [4]. The analysis of these data is not a single classification step, but a sequence of dependent 5

analytical decisions. Quality control determines which cells are retained; normalization and feature 6

selection shape the representation of transcriptional variation; dimensionality reduction and cluster-7

ing define the cell populations to be interpreted; and marker discovery provides the evidence used for 8

cell type annotation. Because each step conditions the next, choices made early in the workflow can 9

propagate to downstream biological interpretation [5, 6]. Cell type annotation is therefore a central 10 

output of scRNA-seq analysis, and its accuracy depends on the quality of the upstream workflow that 11 

produces the clusters and marker evidence. 12

correlation, nearest-neighbor or embedding-based similarity [13–15]. Supervised and deep-learning 17 

methods, including ACTINN, scPred, scDeepSort and transformer-based models, learn decision 18

scFoundation and CellPLM further extend this direction by pre-training on millions of cells across 20 

tissues and conditions, with complementary architectures exploring efficient long-context representa-21 

tion of single-cell profiles [20–22]. More recently, large language models (LLMs) have introduced a 22 

complementary direction by interpreting marker genes and tissue context through natural-language 23 

reasoning. GPTCelltype showed that LLMs can generate biologically plausible cell type labels from 24 

marker lists [23], and CASSIA extended this idea with structured reasoning chains for interpretable 25 

annotation [24]. These methods have advanced automated annotation, but most operate after prepro-26 

cessing and clustering have already been completed. They annotate the output of an existing pipeline 27 

rather than controlling the analytical choices that produce that output. As a result, they cannot 28 

revise upstream decisions when marker evidence is ambiguous, clustering resolution is inappropriate 29 

or intermediate results indicate that the analysis should be adjusted. 30 

This distinction is especially important for LLM-based analysis. Large proprietary models can be 31 

effective at interpreting biological language, but dependence on remote APIs raises concerns about 32 

cost, reproducibility and data governance in settings where genomic data must remain within insti-33 

tutional infrastructure [25, 26]. Open-source small language models, including recent releases of the 34 

Qwen series [27], provide a practical alternative for local deployment, but direct prompting is not 35 

sufficient for long, dependent analytical workflows. A language model asked to label marker genes in 36 

isolation has limited access to the evidence that determines whether those markers are reliable. Agentic 37 

frameworks offer a way to address this limitation by linking language-model reasoning to computa-38 

tional analyses, intermediate observations and the current analytical context, with mechanisms for 39 

tool invocation, chain-of-thought reasoning and verbal self-reflection [28–33]. Such frameworks have 40 

begun to support autonomous research workflows in chemistry [34] and clinical risk prediction [35], 41 

suggesting a similar potential for multi-step single-cell analysis. In single-cell analysis, the relevant 42 

question is therefore not only whether a language model can name a cell type from a gene list, but 43 

whether a structured workflow can guide a small local model through the analytical process that 44 

produces that gene list. We define this strategy as structured workflow orchestration, in which the 45 

current analytical context, established single-cell procedures and intermediate observations jointly 46 

guide decisions from data inspection to final annotation. 47 

Here we present CellPilot, an agentic framework that guides a locally deployable small language 48 

model through the complete scRNA-seq analysis workflow, from raw count matrices to cluster-level 49 

annotation. CellPilot implements structured workflow orchestration by linking a Scanpy-based analyt-50 

ical toolkit with observation-guided reasoning [36]. During each analysis, the model selects analytical 51 

steps, evaluates intermediate results and revises subsequent decisions before producing final annota-52 

tions. We focus on cell type annotation because it integrates the consequences of upstream quality 53 

control, feature selection, dimensionality reduction, clustering and marker discovery into a measur-54 

able biological task. Across GTEx, Tabula Sapiens and Mouse Cell Atlas [37–39], CellPilot achieved 55 

cluster-level annotation accuracies of 0.891, 0.750 and 0.773 under a unified hierarchical evaluation, 56 

exceeding representative reference-based, marker-based and LLM-based annotation methods under 57 

a common reference-cluster scoring protocol. These results show that the workflow produces accu-58 

rate annotations across datasets with different tissue composition, species context and reference-label 59 

structure. In controlled backbone comparisons, the structured workflow substantially reduced depen-60 

dence on model scale: within CellPilot, an open-source 8B model approached GPT-4o performance on 61 

GTEx, whereas the same model performed poorly in a prompt-only annotation setting. CellPilot also 62 

retained complete analytical records and produced confidence scores associated with annotation cor-63 

rectness, supporting prioritization for review. Structured workflow orchestration therefore provides a 64 

practical route for using locally deployable small language models in autonomous cell type annotation 65 

from raw single-cell data. 66

## Results 67 

CellPilot enables autonomous cell type annotation through structured 68 

workflow orchestration 69 

CellPilot is an agentic framework for autonomous cell type annotation from raw single-cell RNA-70 

sequencing data. Unlike LLM-based annotation approaches that assign labels after preprocessing and 71 

clustering have already been completed, CellPilot treats annotation as a workflow-level task whose 72 

accuracy depends on the analytical decisions that generate the evidence for cell identity. Starting 73 

from a raw count matrix, the agent coordinates quality control, normalization, feature selection, 74 

dimensionality reduction, clustering, marker discovery and final cluster-level cell type assignment 75 

within a single traceable session (Fig. 1). This design allows a small language model to reason over 76 

the same intermediate evidence that a human analyst would inspect when annotating single-cell data. 77 

Given an input expression matrix, CellPilot decomposes the annotation process into a sequence 78 

of state-dependent analytical decisions and executes them through an observation-driven reasoning 79 

loop (Fig. 1a,b). At each iteration, the agent selects an analytical function, specifies its parameters, 80 

executes the operation and incorporates the resulting observations into subsequent decisions. The 81 

function library covers the major stages required for annotation, including data inspection, quality 82 

control, filtering, normalization, highly variable gene selection, dimensionality reduction, clustering, 83 

differential expression, marker query, cell type assignment, visualization and reporting (Fig. 1d). A 84 

self-critic module monitors whether the required stages have been completed and returns control to 85 

the agent when additional steps are needed, reducing the risk of premature or incomplete annotation. 86 

CellPilot was designed to guide a locally deployable small language model through structured 87 

analytical evidence rather than depend on a large proprietary model for direct label generation. In this 88 

study, we used Qwen-8B as the default backbone, allowing the annotation workflow to be executed 89 

within local institutional infrastructure without dependence on remote model APIs (Methods). To 90 

incorporate visual evidence commonly used during manual annotation, CellPilot also includes a vision-91 

language module that summarizes quality-control distributions, cluster layouts and marker-expression 92 

plots as textual observations for the agent context (Fig. 1c). These summaries provide auxiliary 93 

evidence for annotation decisions rather than serving as a separate annotation model. 94 

We evaluated CellPilot across GTEx, Tabula Sapiens and Mouse Cell Atlas to test whether struc-95 

tured agent control enables a small language model to perform accurate cell type annotation from 96 

raw single-cell data. The following analyses examine the effect of the agent framework on backbone 97 

sensitivity, compare CellPilot with reference-based, marker-based and LLM-based annotation meth-98 

ods, and characterize the reliability, confidence and execution behavior of the resulting annotation 99 

workflow. 100 

Structured orchestration reduces dependence on backbone scale 101 

A central question for autonomous cell type annotation is whether accurate performance requires a 102 

large proprietary language model, or whether a smaller locally deployable model can achieve com-103 

parable results when guided by a structured analytical workflow. We therefore compared Qwen3-8B 104 

and GPT-4o in two settings: within CellPilot, where the model reasons over workflow state, tool 105 

outputs and intermediate observations, and in GPTCelltype, where the same models perform anno-106 

tation from marker-gene inputs without the CellPilot orchestration framework. Across GTEx, Tabula 107 

Sapiens and Mouse Cell Atlas, CellPilot showed only modest changes in mean hierarchical agreement 108 

when the backbone was switched from Qwen3-8B to GPT-4o, under both score-based and vote-based 109 

aggregation (Fig. 2a). The mean backbone gap, defined as |GPT-4o − Qwen3-8B |, was 0.056 for both 110 

CellPilot aggregation strategies, compared with 0.162 for GPTCelltype (Fig. 2b). Tissue-level paired 111 

comparisons showed the same pattern: CellPilot produced similar annotation scores across back-112 

bones, whereas GPTCelltype remained more sensitive to model scale, particularly in settings where 113 

Qwen3-8B had limited standalone annotation performance (Fig. 2c). 114 

We next quantified how much performance was attributable to the framework itself by comparing 115 

CellPilot with GPTCelltype under the same backbone. The framework gain was positive across most 116 

tissues and datasets, indicating that structured orchestration improved annotation beyond direct 117 

marker-based prompting (Fig. 2d). This gain was larger for Qwen3-8B than for GPT-4o, with mean 118 

tissue-level improvements of 0.35 and 0.19, respectively (paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test, P < 10 −4;119 

Fig. 2e). Thus, the framework provided the greatest benefit when the underlying model had less 120

CellPilot produces accurate cell type annotations across benchmark 129 

datasets 130 

We next evaluated whether CellPilot’s structured annotation workflow produces accurate cell type 131 

assignments across diverse single-cell datasets. Because cell type annotation depends on the preceding 132 

analytical steps that define clusters and marker genes, this comparison was designed to assess the 133 

complete annotation process rather than a single label-assignment prompt. We evaluated GTEx (7 134 

tissues), Tabula Sapiens (24 tissues) and Mouse Cell Atlas (MCA) using the hierarchical scoring 135 

framework of Hou and Ji [23], in which fully correct, partially correct and incorrect predictions receive 136 

scores of 1, 0.5 and 0, respectively (Methods). CellPilot was compared with representative reference-137 

based, marker-based and LLM-based annotation methods, including SingleR, ScType, GPTCelltype4 138 

and CASSIA. These methods differ in their intended input assumptions: cluster-dependent baselines 139 

were evaluated using dataset-provided cluster information, whereas CellPilot started from raw count 140 

matrices and performed preprocessing, clustering, marker discovery and annotation within the same 141 

workflow. To make the comparison consistent, all predictions were scored against the same dataset-142 

defined reference cell types. 143 

CellPilot achieved the highest annotation scores across all three benchmark datasets (Fig. 3a). 144 

Results are reported using majority-vote aggregation (CellPilot-Vote). On GTEx, CellPilot reached 145 

a mean score of 0.891, compared with 0.584 for GPTCelltype4, 0.498 for CASSIA, 0.450 for ScType 146 

and 0.424 for SingleR ( P < 0.001, Wilcoxon rank-sum test). On Tabula Sapiens, CellPilot achieved 147 

0.750, exceeding GPTCelltype4 (0.589), CASSIA (0.569), ScType (0.489) and SingleR (0.446). On 148 

MCA, CellPilot achieved 0.773, compared with 0.508 for GPTCelltype4, 0.476 for CASSIA, 0.383 for 149 

SingleR and 0.273 for ScType. The improvement over reference- and marker-based methods was most 150 

pronounced in MCA, where cross-species annotation and uneven marker coverage pose challenges for 151 

methods that rely more directly on reference mapping or fixed marker dictionaries. 152 

CellPilot’s performance was also consistent across individual tissues rather than being driven by a 153 

small number of favorable cases (Fig. 3b). In GTEx, CellPilot ranked highest across all seven tissues, 154 

maintaining high agreement across tissues with different cellular compositions. In Tabula Sapiens, 155 

CellPilot performed strongly across both immune-rich tissues, such as Blood and Bone Marrow, and 156 

more heterogeneous epithelial tissues, such as Lung and Large Intestine. Competing methods showed 157 

larger tissue-to-tissue variation, particularly in tissues with broader cellular diversity or closely related 158 

cell states. These results indicate that CellPilot’s annotation gains are not restricted to a single atlas 159 

or tissue context, but are preserved across human and mouse datasets with different annotation 160 

granularity, tissue composition and reference-label structure. 161 

CellPilot annotations remain biologically coherent beyond exact agreement 162 

Hierarchical annotation scores provide a standardized measure of agreement, but they do not fully 163 

capture the biological distance between an incorrect prediction and the reference label. This distinc-164 

tion is important in cell type annotation, where labels often differ in granularity even when they refer 165 

to closely related identities within the Cell Ontology hierarchy. We therefore examined whether non-166 

exact CellPilot annotations were biologically close to the reference labels by computing SapBERT 167 

cosine similarity for predictions scored as partial matches or mismatches (Fig. 3c). 168 

Across GTEx, MCA and Tabula Sapiens, CellPilot predictions that did not receive full credit 169 

showed higher semantic similarity to the reference labels than the corresponding predictions from 170 

competing methods. This pattern was observed for both partial matches and complete mismatches, 171 

indicating that CellPilot errors were more often near misses within related cell identities rather than 172 

unrelated label assignments. For example, a prediction of “CD4 + T cells” for a reference label of “T 173 

helper cells” is penalized under exact ontology agreement, but still reflects a closely related immune-174 

cell identity. Similar trends were observed when each baseline method was examined separately 175

Cell-level annotation performance varies with annotation granularity and 188 

cluster size 189 

After establishing the overall cluster-level accuracy of CellPilot, we examined how annotation perfor-190 

mance varied across individual cells, label granularity and cluster size, which together determine the 191 

strength and specificity of the evidence available for cell type assignment. Across 694,701 annotated 192 

cells in GTEx and Tabula Sapiens, 79.2% of cells received fully matched annotations, 14.5% received 193 

partially matched annotations and 6.3% were mismatched (Fig. 4a). These results indicate that most 194 

cells were assigned labels that agreed with the reference annotation under the Cell Ontology hierarchy. 195 

Annotation accuracy was influenced by the biological resolution of the reference labels. Major cell 196 

types were annotated with a full-match rate of 83%, compared with 76% for cell subtypes (Fig. 4b), 197 

consistent with the greater marker overlap and narrower transcriptional boundaries that distinguish 198 

closely related subpopulations. To assess whether errors were concentrated within particular lineages, 199 

we further mapped labels to broad cell type categories and examined the ten most frequent cate-200 

gories in human and mouse datasets (Supplementary Note 7, Supplementary Figs. 4–5). Structurally 201 

well-defined lineages, including fibroblasts and endothelial cells, showed consistently high agreement, 202 

whereas more transcriptionally overlapping populations, such as myeloid and macrophage/monocyte 203 

categories, showed higher rates of within-lineage confusion. Thus, most broad-category errors reflected 204 

ambiguity among related cell identities rather than arbitrary reassignment across unrelated lineages. 205 

Cluster size provided an additional source of variation in annotation quality. Large clusters con-206 

taining more than 100 cells achieved an 80% full-match rate, whereas medium clusters containing 207 

10–100 cells and small clusters containing 10 or fewer cells achieved full-match rates of 47% and 35%, 208 

respectively (Fig. 4c). This pattern is expected because small clusters provide weaker differential-209 

expression evidence and less stable marker-gene rankings for annotation. At the tissue level, agreement 210 

proportions also varied across organ systems, with immune-rich tissues such as Bone Marrow and 211 

Lymph Node showing high full-match rates, whereas tissues with more heterogeneous or glandular 212 

compositions showed higher proportions of partial matches (Fig. 4d). Consistent with this interpre-213 

tation, tissue-level cell type diversity was only weakly associated with mean agreement but showed 214 

a stronger negative association with predicted confidence (Supplementary Fig. 6). These analyses 215 

define the operating characteristics of CellPilot: annotation is most reliable when cluster-level marker 216 

evidence is strong, whereas small or compositionally complex cell populations should be prioritized 217 

for review. 218 

Confidence scores and visual evidence support annotation assessment 219 

Because autonomous annotation should indicate which predictions warrant additional inspection, we 220 

next assessed whether CellPilot confidence scores reflected annotation reliability. CellPilot assigns 221 

each cluster annotation a confidence score based on the relative support of marker genes for the 222 

predicted identity (Methods). At the tissue level, mean predicted confidence was positively associated 223 

with mean agreement score in both GTEx ( R2 = 0 .24) and Tabula Sapiens ( R2 = 0 .29; Fig. 4e). 224 

Although these correlations do not imply perfect calibration, they indicate that confidence scores 225 

capture useful variation in annotation quality across tissues. Calibration analysis further showed 226 

a tendency toward underconfidence at intermediate score ranges, with observed full-match rates 227 

often exceeding predicted confidence (Supplementary Fig. 7a). Confidence distributions differed across 228 

datasets, particularly in MCA, where broader and more multimodal scores reflected greater variation 229 

in cluster size and cell type composition (Supplementary Fig. 7b). 230

CellPilot executes multi-step workflows with traceable and recoverable 251 

behavior 252 

A practical annotation agent must not only produce accurate labels, but also complete the analytical 253 

steps that lead to those labels without requiring manual intervention. We therefore examined whether 254 

CellPilot could execute the full annotation workflow reliably across benchmark sessions, from raw 255 

data inspection to final cell type assignment and reporting. Workflow completion rates were high 256 

across datasets, reaching 98.1% in Tabula Sapiens and 96.9% in MCA, with a lower but still robust 257 

completion rate of 88.1% in GTEx (Fig. 4h). The lower GTEx rate reflected greater heterogeneity 258 

in input formatting and data-quality conventions across tissue contributions, rather than a single 259 

recurrent failure mode. Across all completed sessions, CellPilot required a mean of 17 .0 ± 2.3 tool 260 

calls, indicating that the agent followed a bounded sequence of analytical steps rather than repeatedly 261 

invoking tools without convergence (Fig. 4i). 262 

Step-level analysis showed that most core single-cell operations were executed with high reliabil-263 

ity (Fig. 4g). Deterministic analytical steps, including normalization, highly variable gene selection, 264 

dimensionality reduction, neighborhood graph construction, clustering, visualization and report 265 

generation, succeeded in nearly all sessions. Steps that depend more strongly on dataset-specific prop-266 

erties showed lower success rates. Quality-control filtering was less stable when datasets had unusual 267 

metric distributions that did not match standard filtering heuristics, and marker-query failures were 268 

concentrated in rare or poorly characterized populations with limited marker database coverage. The 269 

LLM annotation step was invoked only for clusters that remained unresolved after marker-based 270 

matching; failures in this step therefore reflected ambiguous marker evidence rather than failure of 271 

the primary annotation path. 272 

Importantly, individual tool failures did not necessarily terminate the annotation workflow. When 273 

a tool call failed, CellPilot received the error as an observation and used it to revise the subse-274 

quent analysis, either by adjusting parameters, returning to an earlier step or selecting an alternative 275 

operation. Clusters that remained unresolved after marker-based and LLM-assisted annotation were 276 

explicitly flagged for review rather than assigned a low-confidence label without indication. This 277 

behavior is central to the design of CellPilot: annotation decisions are accompanied by intermediate 278 

observations, confidence information and recoverable execution traces. Together, these results show 279 

that CellPilot functions as a traceable annotation workflow rather than a single-pass labeling prompt, 280 

allowing both successful assignments and unresolved cases to be inspected after execution. 281

Case study: annotating a keratinocyte transformation trajectory from 282 

normal skin to actinic keratosis and cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma 283 

Single-cell RNA sequencing has been widely applied to characterize the cellular ecosystems of 284 

cutaneous and head-and-neck squamous tumours, revealing extensive intra-tumoural cell-state het-285 

erogeneity that challenges manual annotation [40, 41]. To evaluate whether autonomous cell-type 286 

annotation can support biologically meaningful interpretation in such settings, we applied CellPilot to 287 

a publicly available single-cell RNA sequencing dataset spanning normal skin (NS), actinic keratosis 288 

(AK), and cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) [42]. The dataset comprises nine samples with 289 

three biological replicates per condition and presents a challenging setting with heterogeneous cellular 290 

composition, disease-associated transcriptional remodeling, and no ground-truth annotations. Each 291 

sample was processed independently using our Qwen-8B-powered agent without manual curation, 292 

followed by integration using PCA and Harmony [43] for batch correction in a shared UMAP space. 293 

The integrated embedding revealed coherent epithelial, stromal, vascular, and immune compartments 294 

that were consistently recovered across independently processed samples (Fig. 5a). 295 

Annotation validity was first assessed through canonical marker expression, which showed strong 296 

cell-type specificity with minimal off-target signal (Fig. 5b). Beyond major cell populations, the agent 297 

autonomously identified a rare Langerhans cell population that was not explicitly included in the 298 

provided annotation prior. Importantly, the agent was initialized only with common skin cell markers 299 

and was not supplied with Langerhans-specific marker genes, curated references, or predefined label 300 

vocabularies containing this population. The inferred identity was subsequently validated through 301 

examination of lineage-specific markers, including CD207, which showed mutually exclusive expression 302 

relative to neighboring immune populations such as macrophages and dendritic cells (Fig. 5c). 303 

This result highlights a key limitation of conventional annotation workflows. Most existing 304 

approaches rely on predefined references, curated marker lists, or closed label spaces, and therefore 305 

primarily recover cell identities already represented in prior knowledge. In contrast, the agent was 306 

able to propose a biologically valid but non-obvious population outside the explicitly provided annota-307 

tion space. Notably, Langerhans cells were embedded within a closely related immune transcriptional 308 

neighborhood, making their identification nontrivial even for experienced analysts without domain-309 

specific prior familiarity. Rather than reflecting only increased clustering granularity, this finding 310 

demonstrates that autonomous agentic reasoning can expand the candidate biological hypothesis 311 

space beyond the analyst’s initial assumptions. 312 

We next assessed whether these annotations supported downstream systems-level analysis. 313 

Reconstruction of cell–cell communication networks using CellChat [44] across NS, AK, and SCC 314 

revealed disease-associated interaction remodeling consistent with progressive epithelial transforma-315 

tion (Fig. 5d). Together, these results suggest that agent-derived annotations are biologically coherent, 316 

reproducible across independently processed samples, and sufficiently robust to support downstream 317 

analyses without manual curation. 318 

## Discussion 319 

CellPilot was developed for autonomous cell type annotation from raw single-cell RNA-sequencing 320 

data. Its central premise is that annotation should not be treated as a terminal label-assignment step, 321 

because the evidence used to assign cell identity is shaped by upstream choices in quality control, 322 

normalization, feature selection, clustering and marker discovery. CellPilot links these decisions within 323 

a state-machine-controlled agentic workflow, allowing a locally deployable small language model to 324 

inspect intermediate results, execute single-cell analysis tools and revise subsequent decisions before 325 

producing cluster-level annotations. This design differs from prompt-based annotation methods that 326 

operate on marker lists after preprocessing and clustering have already been completed. 327 

The main methodological finding is that workflow orchestration can substantially reduce depen-328 

dence on backbone model scale in autonomous cell type annotation. Under direct prompt-based 329 

annotation, Qwen3-8B remained markedly behind GPT-4o, whereas within CellPilot the performance 330 

gap between the two models was much smaller across the three benchmark datasets under both score-331 

and vote-based aggregation. The framework gain was also larger for Qwen3-8B than for GPT-4o, 332 

indicating that orchestration is most beneficial when the underlying model has limited standalone 333 

annotation capacity. Accurate annotation, in this setting, does not arise simply from using a larger 334 

language model. It is supported by constraining model reasoning with workflow context, executable 335 

tools and biological observations generated during the analysis. 336

CellPilot produced accurate annotations across GTEx, Tabula Sapiens and Mouse Cell Atlas, out-337 

performing representative reference-based, marker-based and LLM-based annotation methods under 338 

a unified hierarchical evaluation. These methods represent distinct strategies for automated annota-339 

tion: mapping query profiles to reference atlases, matching marker signatures or using language models 340 

to interpret marker genes. CellPilot addresses the same task from a different angle, by coordinating 341 

the upstream analysis that produces the clusters and marker evidence used for label assignment. Its 342 

advantage was evident not only in aggregate agreement but also in the behavior of non-exact predic-343 

tions. Semantic analysis showed that CellPilot annotations that did not exactly match the reference 344 

labels were often close to the correct identity within the Cell Ontology hierarchy, and UpSet analysis 345 

showed that CellPilot contributed a large number of uniquely correct annotations. Thus, the frame-346 

work improved annotation quality beyond increasing the number of exact matches, yielding errors 347 

that were more biologically coherent and resolving labels missed by existing strategies. 348 

The practical value of CellPilot depends on more than annotation accuracy. Autonomous anno-349 

tation should also expose uncertainty, recover from failures and leave an analytical record that 350 

can be inspected after execution. CellPilot addresses these requirements through confidence scores, 351 

structured observations and recoverable tool execution. The confidence scores were associated with 352 

annotation correctness and supported prioritization of predictions for review, although they should 353 

not be interpreted as perfectly calibrated probabilities. Transparent reporting of uncertainty and 354 

execution traces is consistent with emerging reporting standards for studies that use large language 355 

models in biomedical settings [45]. The vision-language module provided additional visual evidence 356 

in a context-dependent manner, with the largest gains in MCA and smaller or negligible effects in 357 

several human tissues. Confidence estimation and visual interpretation therefore serve as supporting 358 

mechanisms for annotation assessment, rather than independent sources of method novelty. 359 

Several limitations remain. CellPilot performed less well on very small clusters, where differential-360 

expression evidence is unstable and marker-gene rankings are less reliable. This limitation is not 361 

specific to language models, but reflects a broader challenge in annotating rare or sparsely sam-362 

pled populations from weak cluster-level evidence. Pan-cancer analyses have further shown that 363 

intra-tumoural cell-state heterogeneity is a pervasive feature across tumour types [46], motivating 364 

annotation methods that remain robust under such variation. Performance was also lower on MCA 365 

than on the human datasets, consistent with the greater heterogeneity of the mouse atlas and less 366 

complete marker coverage for some mouse cell types. Incorporating species-specific marker resources 367 

and stronger strategies for rare-cell annotation may further improve performance. The contribution 368 

of visual reasoning also varied across tissues, suggesting that future versions should determine when 369 

visual evidence is likely to be informative rather than applying it uniformly. 370 

The skin cancer case study illustrates CellPilot not merely as an automated annotation pipeline, 371 

but as a framework for autonomous biological interpretation. Starting from raw expression profiles, 372 

the agent independently recovered coherent cellular populations, including a rare Langerhans cell 373 

population that was not explicitly represented in the provided annotation prior. Importantly, this 374 

should not be interpreted as a claim of novel biological discovery, but rather as evidence that agen-375 

tic reasoning can recover biologically valid and non-obvious hypotheses beyond the analyst’s initial 376 

assumptions. The resulting annotations further supported downstream analyses of cellular communi-377 

cation, suggesting that the inferred cellular identities were sufficiently coherent to enable systems-level 378 

biological interpretation without manual curation. 379 

## Methods 380 

CellPilot workflow formulation 381 

CellPilot was designed to execute single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis as a sequence of 382 

state-dependent analytical decisions rather than as a terminal cell type labeling step. The input to 383 

CellPilot is a raw or minimally processed gene expression matrix, denoted by X ∈ Rn×p, where n384 

is the number of cells and p is the number of genes. Input files were provided in AnnData ( .h5ad )385 

or numeric matrix ( .csv ) format. A species identifier, tissue context and a marker-gene reference 386 

table could be supplied when available. The output of one CellPilot session consists of cluster-level 387 

cell type annotations, annotation support scores, intermediate analysis objects, diagnostic figures and 388 

structured execution logs. 389

State-machine constrained agent orchestration 411 

CellPilot was implemented as a state-machine controlled agent using LangGraph. The state object 412 

contained the dataset path, session directory, current AnnData file, message history, execution 413 

counter, completed workflow stages, tool-call records and returned observations. The state machine 414 

constrained the agent to select one validated analytical action at a time. 415 

The language model controller generated actions using a restricted XML interface. A tool call was 416 

required to appear inside an <execute> block, and a final response was required to appear inside a 417 

<solution> block. Tool outputs were returned to the controller as structured <observation> blocks. 418 

At each iteration, the generated XML was parsed and checked before execution. Invalid XML, missing 419 

arguments or unsupported tool names were returned as structured error observations, allowing the 420 

agent to revise the next action without terminating the session. 421 

Tool calls were executed through predefined Python wrappers with restricted arguments and 422 

session-local file paths. Each wrapper returned a structured observation containing a textual summary, 423 

output file paths when applicable, and an execution status. Runtime exceptions were captured and 424 

returned as error observations. These errors were not treated as session-level failures unless the agent 425 

reached the iteration limit or failed to produce the required final artifacts. 426 

A self-checking step was applied before accepting a final <solution> block. This step checked 427 

whether all required workflow stages had been completed at least once or had been explicitly skipped 428 

with a valid reason in the action log. If required stages were missing, the final response was rejected 429 

and control returned to the agent. The self-checking step evaluated workflow completeness only; it 430 

did not use reference labels and did not judge biological correctness of the predicted annotations. 431 

Because scRNA-seq workflows can generate long textual and visual outputs, observations were 432 

compressed before being appended to the active model context. The full observations and out-433 

put files were retained in the session directory, whereas the active reasoning context retained only 434 

bounded summaries of recent observations. The maximum observation length and number of retained 435 

observations used in the experiments are reported in Supplementary Note 1. 436

Language model controllers 437 

Qwen3-8B [27] was used as the default language model controller in CellPilot. The model was deployed 438 

locally through the vLLM inference engine [47]. The controller did not directly manipulate the expres-439 

sion matrix. Instead, it selected from the fixed tool set described in Section 1, interpreted structured 440 

observations and decided whether additional analysis steps were required. 441 

Each session used a fixed prompt template containing the task description, available tool schemas, 442 

XML response rules, required workflow stages and constraints on final output format. The prompt 443 

instructed the controller to inspect data properties, quality-control metrics, dimensionality-reduction 444 

results, clustering structure and marker evidence before producing the final annotation report. To 445 

reduce output variability, inference used a fixed sampling temperature of τ = 0 .1 across experi-446 

ments. The model name, checkpoint, serving configuration, decoding parameters and context limits 447 

are described in Supplementary Note 3. 448 

For backbone comparison experiments, the same CellPilot workflow, tool library, prompt tem-449 

plate and evaluation protocol were used with Qwen3-8B and GPT-4o. For prompt-only comparisons, 450 

GPTCelltype was run using marker-gene inputs without the CellPilot state machine, tool execution 451 

loop or intermediate observations. API-based models were queried with fixed prompts, and the model 452 

version and access date are reported in Supplementary Note 3. 453 

Atomic tool library and workflow stages 454 

CellPilot used a library of atomic tools implemented around Scanpy and AnnData. Each tool read 455 

the current AnnData object, performed one analytical operation, wrote updated objects or figures to 456 

the session directory, and returned a structured observation to the agent. The tool library contained 457 

the following functional groups. 458 

Data inspection and quality control. 459 

The input matrix was loaded into an AnnData object. Cell and gene counts, total counts per cell 460 

and the fraction of mitochondrial transcripts were computed. Mitochondrial genes were detected 461 

using species-specific prefixes. Quality-control plots were generated for gene counts, total counts and 462 

mitochondrial content. Filtering thresholds were selected by predefined quantile-based heuristics with 463 

a conservative retention rule that preserved at least 90% of cells unless the data contained clear 464 

low-quality tails. Exact default thresholds and fallback rules are described in Supplementary Note 2. 465 

Normalization and feature selection. 466 

Filtered count matrices were library-size normalized and log transformed. Highly variable genes were 467 

selected using the default Scanpy procedure. If dispersion-based selection failed or returned an unsta-468 

ble feature set, the workflow invoked a Seurat v3-style fallback [48]. The number of highly variable 469 

genes, normalization target, minimum gene filters and fallback criteria are described in Supplementary 470 

Note 2. 471 

Dimensionality reduction and graph construction. 472 

Principal component analysis was performed on the selected highly variable genes using a fixed 473 

random seed. A neighborhood graph was constructed from the top principal components, followed by 474 

UMAP projection for visualization [49]. The number of principal components, number of neighbors, 475 

distance metric and random seed are described in Supplementary Note 2. 476 

Clustering. 477 

Graph-based clustering was performed using the Leiden algorithm [50]. Cluster labels were stored 478 

in the AnnData object and used for downstream differential expression and annotation. The default 479 

resolution and any agent-selected resolution adjustments were recorded in the session log. The agent 480 

was allowed to repeat clustering with a modified resolution when the returned observations indicated 481 

severe over-clustering, under-clustering or poor separation in the diagnostic plots. 482 

Differential expression and marker extraction. 483 

For each cluster, differentially expressed genes were computed against the remaining cells using rank-484 

based testing as implemented in Scanpy, consistent with established practice in single-cell differential 485 

expression analysis [51, 52]. The top-ranked genes for each cluster were written to the AnnData object 486

Visual observation enrichment 506 

CellPilot optionally used a vision-language module to convert diagnostic figures into textual observa-507 

tions. Vision-language models combine large-language-model reasoning with visual encoders trained 508 

to interpret images alongside textual instructions [53, 54]. The module was applied to figures gener-509 

ated during the workflow, including quality-control violin plots, principal component variance plots, 510 

UMAP embeddings, marker dot plots and feature plots. In the experiments using visual observations, 511 

Qwen3-8B-VL-Thinking was used as the visual summarization model. 512 

For each figure, the module generated a structured textual summary of salient visual patterns. 513 

Quality-control summaries described distributional features such as extreme tails or high mitochon-514 

drial content. Embedding summaries described cluster separation and visually ambiguous regions. 515 

Marker-expression summaries described cluster specificity and co-expression patterns. These sum-516 

maries were appended to the agent state as auxiliary observations. The VLM module did not assign 517 

cell type labels directly and did not modify numerical tool outputs. 518 

Figure summaries were cached by file path and metadata to avoid repeated processing of the same 519 

image. If visual summarization failed or returned invalid text, an empty observation was recorded 520 

and the workflow continued. Ablation experiments disabled this module while keeping all other tool 521 

calls, prompts and evaluation procedures unchanged. 522

Baseline methods and input assumptions 537 

CellPilot was compared with representative reference-based, marker-based and LLM-based annota-538 

tion methods. Because these methods use different input assumptions, we separated method inputs 539 

from evaluation units. Cluster-dependent baselines were evaluated under their intended pre-defined 540 

cluster settings, whereas CellPilot performed de novo preprocessing, clustering, marker discovery and 541 

annotation from raw count matrices. All predictions were subsequently evaluated against the same 542 

dataset-defined reference annotations. 543 

a. SingleR [13]: SingleR was run as a reference-based cell-level annotation method. It assigned labels 544 

by comparing query profiles with reference expression signatures using correlation-based scoring. 545 

Cell-level predictions were aggregated to the reference-cluster level by majority vote. 546 

b. ScType [12]: ScType was run as a marker-based cluster annotation method using curated marker 547 

gene sets. For datasets where tissue-specific marker references were available, ScType was applied 548 

to the dataset-provided cluster structure. 549 

c. GPTCelltype4 [23]: GPTCelltype4 was run as a prompt-based LLM annotation baseline using 550 

differential gene lists from pre-defined clusters. The marker size parameter was set to 10 following 551 

the original protocol, and tissue context was supplied according to the dataset. 552 

d. CASSIA [24]: CASSIA was run as a structured LLM-based annotation baseline using marker 553 

genes and tissue context. Default parameters were used unless otherwise specified by the original 554 

implementation. 555 

No manual correction, relabeling or post hoc filtering was applied to baseline outputs. The input 556 

data, cluster assumptions, marker sources, model versions and runtime settings for each method are 557 

summarized in Supplementary Note 5. 558 

Evaluation alignment for de novo and reference clusters 559 

CellPilot produces labels on de novo clusters, whereas several baselines operate on dataset-defined 560 

reference clusters. To evaluate all methods on a common unit, we projected method outputs onto the 561 

reference clusters provided by each benchmark dataset. 562 

For CellPilot, every cell inherited the predicted label of its CellPilot cluster. These cell-level labels 563 

were then grouped by the dataset-defined reference cluster. Two aggregation schemes were used. 564 

In the reference-cluster vote scheme, the modal CellPilot label among cells in a reference cluster 565 

was selected as the predicted label for that reference cluster. In the reference-cluster mean-score 566 

scheme, each cell first received an ontology score according to the label of its CellPilot cluster, and 567 

scores were then averaged within each reference cluster. For baselines that directly returned labels 568 

on reference clusters, the returned cluster label was scored directly. For SingleR, cell-level predictions 569 

were aggregated within each reference cluster by majority vote before scoring. 570 

This alignment used reference clusters only for evaluation. CellPilot did not use reference clusters, 571 

reference marker lists or reference labels during workflow execution. 572

Support-score assessment 603 

The relationship between CellPilot support scores and annotation correctness was evaluated after 604 

reference-cluster alignment. For each tissue, mean predicted support was computed across reference 605 

clusters, and compared with the corresponding tissue-level ontology score. Pearson correlation coef-606 

ficients were reported as descriptive measures. Support-score threshold analysis was performed by 607 

retaining predictions with C(c) above a threshold and computing the full-match rate and retained-608 

cluster fraction at each threshold. These analyses were used to evaluate whether support scores 609 

could prioritize annotations for review. They were not used to treat support scores as calibrated 610 

probabilities. 611

The modular toolkit supports major stages of single-cell analysis, including quality control, filtering, highly variable gene identification, normalization, dimensionality reduction, clustering, differential expression, visualization, marker gene identification, data integration, cell-type assignment, and reporting.

1.1 State Fields Beyond the Core Architecture 863 

The AgentState object described in Section 4.2 is implemented as a Python TypedDict containing 864 

seven fields. In addition to the five fields noted in the main text (user query, current dataset path, 865 

session-specific working directory, message history, execution counter, and tools-executed record), two 866 

additional fields support workflow control: plan generated , a boolean flag that distinguishes the 867 

initial planning turn from subsequent execution turns to prevent the routing logic from misinterpreting 868 

a plan-only response as a stalled state; and session id , a UUID-based identifier used to construct 869 

the session directory path and to tag logs for multi-session deployments. 870 

1.2 LangGraph Implementation and Routing Details 871 

The state-machine control loop described in Section 4.2 and Algorithm 1 is compiled via LangGraph’s 872 

StateGraph API into a directed graph with conditional edges. Two routing details not captured by 873 

Algorithm 1 are relevant for reproducibility. First, the execute node is connected back to generate 874 

via a fixed unconditional edge rather than a conditional one, which guarantees that every tool invoca-875 

tion is followed by a new LLM reasoning step. There is no execution path in which two consecutive tool 876 

calls occur without an intervening reasoning iteration. Second, the self-critic node is inserted between 877 

the generate node and the terminal END node only when the critic mechanism is enabled. When dis-878 

abled, the <solution> directive transitions directly to END , reducing latency by one additional LLM 879 

call per session. 880 

1.3 Execution Sandboxing and Path Resolution 881 

Tool calls are executed within a sandboxed environment managed by the ExecutionEngine class, a 882 

subsystem not described in the main text. Before each execution, the working directory is changed 883 

to the session-specific directory via os.chdir , ensuring that all relative file paths resolve within 884 

the session scope. Upon completion or failure, the original working directory is restored. File paths 885 

appearing in generated code are automatically rewritten to absolute paths using a regex-based resolver 886 

that checks for the existence of candidate files first in the session directory and then in the global 887 

working directory. This path rewriting prevents file-not-found errors that would otherwise arise when 888 

the language model generates code referencing filenames without full paths. 889 

Tool outputs are captured by redirecting stdout and stderr into string buffers, intercepting 890 

any generated image files detected either through regex matching of file extensions in output text 891 

or through explicit matplotlib.Figure objects in the execution namespace, copying images into a 892 

figures/ subdirectory under the session directory with UUID-prefixed filenames to avoid collisions, 893 

and returning a standardized result dictionary containing an ok boolean, the captured output text, 894 

a list of image records, and an updated data path if the tool modified the AnnData object. 895

1.5 Context Window Management: Parameter Specifications 923 

Section 4.2 of the main text describes the general context management strategy, including observation 924 

truncation and bounded retention of recent observations. Here we specify the three-level truncation 925 

hierarchy and its default parameter values as implemented. 926 

At the observation level , individual tool outputs undergo two sequential truncations: first by 927 

line count (default: 150 lines retained from the head, plus the last 5 lines with an inserted skip 928 

marker indicating the number of omitted lines), and subsequently by character count (default: 2,000 929 

characters, with a trailing truncation marker). This dual truncation preserves key summary statistics 930 

at the beginning of tool outputs and tail-end status information while discarding verbose intermediate 931 

content. 932 

At the history level , a category-aware pruning method operates before each LLM invocation, 933 

processing the message history in reverse chronological order and independently limiting three message 934 

categories: observation messages (those containing <observation> tags, capped at the 6 most recent), 935 

AI reasoning messages (capped at the 8 most recent), and user messages (capped at the 3 most recent 936 

turns). The two most recent messages are always retained regardless of category limits. The total 937 

message count is bounded at 40 messages. All parameters are configurable at agent initialization and 938 

can be adjusted for language models with different context window sizes. 939 

At the VLM summary level , image summaries are constrained to 100 words each, enforced by post 940 

hoc word-count truncation even if the VLM exceeds the prompt instruction, with at most 2 image 941 

summaries generated per tool invocation. 942 

1.6 Error Handling: Specific Safeguards 943 

Section 4.2 describes the general error recovery strategy in which failures are returned as structured 944 

observations that allow the model to revise its plan. Here we document three additional safeguards 945 

implemented in the code. 946 

First, a global recursion limit of 100 LangGraph iterations is set via the recursion limit config-947 

uration parameter. This prevents infinite cycling in edge cases where the model repeatedly generates 948 

unparseable or non-progressing outputs. 949 

Second, a secondary safety check within the generate node forces session termination with an 950 

automatic <solution> message if the execution counter exceeds 50 tool invocations, providing a 951 

tighter upper bound than the graph-level recursion limit. 952 

Third, when a <solution> tag is emitted, the agent verifies that all required tools 953 

have been executed by checking the tools executed list against a required set that cur-954 

rently includes tool validate annotation . Because this validation step internally invokes 955 

tool generate annotation report , the final quality check and report generation are coupled. If 956 

the requirement is not satisfied, the proposed solution is intercepted, a reminder is injected into the 957 

conversation history, and control returns to generate .958

3.6 Prompt Design Rationale 1121 

Several design choices in the prompt system merit explicit justification, as they reflect trade-offs 1122 

specific to piloting compact language models through multi-step analytical workflows. 1123 

Redundant constraint encoding. The preprocessing order (normalize → log → HVG → scale →1124 

PCA) is stated repeatedly in the system prompt, including a numbered sequence, a negative rule, and 1125 

a worked code example. This redundancy is intended to reduce order violations in long multi-step 1126 

sessions. 1127 

Checklist-driven planning. The prompt requires the agent to produce an explicit analysis plan 1128 

with checkboxes in its first response and to mark each item as completed or skipped with justification 1129 

before emitting a <solution> . This externalizes progress tracking into the conversation history. 1130 

Data-conditional threshold guidance. Rather than prescribing fixed QC thresholds, the prompt 1131 

provides a mapping from observed quantile statistics to plausible threshold ranges. This allows the 1132 

agent to adapt parameter choices to the structure of the input dataset. 1133 

Single-execute constraint. Restricting the model to one <execute> block per turn forces the 1134 

agent to observe each tool output before planning the next step, producing a cleaner thought-action-1135 

observation loop. 1136

This note describes the origin of the marker information used by CellPilot, the scoring logic imple-1314

6.1 Marker Source Hierarchy 1318 

CellPilot first allows the user to provide a custom marker gene list. This is the preferred mode when 1319 

the user has access to a curated tissue-specific or study-specific marker resource. When no user-1320 

supplied marker file is available, the system falls back to an internal default marker database derived 1321 

from PanglaoDB. 1322 

This design separates two use cases. In one setting, CellPilot serves as a flexible annotation 1323 

framework that can incorporate expert-provided prior knowledge. In the other, it operates in a default 1324 

mode using a broad public marker resource. 1325 

6.2 Marker Query Procedure 1326 

The core marker-based annotation logic is implemented in the tool query markers function in 1327 

marker db.py . For each cluster, the tool extracts the top K differentially expressed genes from 1328 

rank genes groups , with top k=50 by default. Gene symbols are converted to uppercase before 1329 

matching to the marker database. If a queried gene is found in the marker table for one or more cell 1330 

types, the hit count for the corresponding cell type is incremented. This procedure yields, for each 1331 

cluster, a dictionary of the form {cell type: hit count }.1332 

The predicted cell type for the cluster is defined as the cell type with the highest hit count. 1333 

6.3 Marker-Based Confidence Score 1334 

The confidence score used in the primary marker-based annotation layer is defined as 1335 

confidence = top hit count total hits for cluster .

Thus, if a cluster produces 20 total marker hits across all candidate cell types and 15 of those 1336 

hits support a T-cell label, the resulting confidence score is 15 /20 = 0 .75. This score reflects the 1337 

concentration of marker evidence on the top-supported label rather than the absolute number of 1338 

matched markers. 1339

6.7 Hierarchical Evaluation Rules 1360 

Each identified cell type annotation was assigned an unambiguous cell ontology name and, when 1361

7.3 Cluster Size Effects 1429 

The dependence of annotation accuracy on cluster size reflects a fundamental constraint of differential 1430 

expression analysis. In clusters of ten or fewer cells, the statistical power to identify stable and 1431 

reproducible marker rankings is limited, and the resulting DEG lists are more sensitive to sampling 1432 

noise. Under these conditions, the genes passed to the annotation step may vary substantially across 1433 

runs and may not faithfully represent the canonical marker structure of the underlying cell type. This 1434 

instability propagates directly into annotation quality. 1435 

In practical terms, very small clusters warrant closer manual review regardless of the annotation 1436 

tool used. CellPilot confidence scores offer one practical way to flag such cases for further inspection 1437 

without requiring a fixed size cutoff in advance. 1438

Tissues with predominantly epithelial or stromal compositions showed higher proportions of partial 1445 

matches. Mammary and Kidney were notable in this regard, reflecting the transcriptional similarity 1446 

among epithelial and stromal subtypes that complicates boundary assignment at fine resolution. 1447 

Skeletal muscle, Muscle, and Heart showed intermediate performance; muscle lineage markers are 1448 

moderately specific, but subtype boundaries vary across tissue contexts and annotation schemes, 1449 

contributing more often to partial rather than full agreement. 1450 

These tissue-dependent patterns support the practical use of confidence-based filtering rather than 1451 

a single global threshold. Tissues that are intrinsically more difficult to annotate can be expected 1452 

to produce lower-confidence predictions on average, allowing review effort to be allocated more 1453 

selectively. 1454
