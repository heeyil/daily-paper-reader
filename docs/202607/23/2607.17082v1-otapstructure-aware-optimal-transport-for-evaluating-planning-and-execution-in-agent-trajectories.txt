Abstract 

Large language model agents solve tasks by generating tra-jectories that interleave planning, tool calls, and intermediate results. Current evaluation metrics reduce such a trajectory to a binary success flag or compare it against a reference by exact matching. A success flag cannot distinguish a sound solution from one that succeeds by luck, and says nothing about why a failed run went wrong. Exact matching penal-izes plans that are valid but reordered or decomposed dif-ferently from the reference. We reframe trajectory evaluation as a distance between the agent’s execution graph and a set of valid solution graphs, and instantiate it via an unbalanced fused Gromov-Wasserstein transport problem over attributed dependency graphs. The resulting score, termed Otap (Opti-mal Transport for Agentic Planning), is a pseudo-metric that is provably invariant to dependency-preserving reorderings and has bounded sensitivity to redundant steps. Its unbal-anced marginals handle missing or hallucinated steps without forcing a match, and its soft coupling accommodates varia-tion in plan granularity. On controlled perturbations and three public benchmarks, Otap separates valid from invalid trajec-tories in a regime where semantics-only metrics score below chance. Its accuracy is highest when the dependency graph is recovered exactly, and drops only when the graph is inferred heuristically from free-text traces. 

# 1 Introduction 

Agentic systems built on large language models (LLMs) de-compose open-ended natural language goals into distinct ex-ecution trajectories. These trajectories interleave high-level planning, explicit tool calls, and dynamic environment in-teractions to manage intermediate execution states. Contem-porary frameworks span explicit plan-and-execute pipelines, ReAct-style interleaved reasoning and acting pipelines (Yao et al. 2023a), and tree- or graph-structured deliberation graphs (Yao et al. 2023b). As agent architectures increasingly transition into production environments, establishing rigor-ous, process-level evaluation methodologies has become a primary bottleneck for both academic research and system-atic deployment. Current agent evaluation metrics generally fall into three paradigms, each exhibiting distinct limitations:

• Outcome-only evaluation (e.g., end-task success rates in benchmarks like AgentBench (Liu et al. 2024), GAIA (Mi-alon et al. 2024), or WebArena (Zhou et al. 2024)) offers objective quantification but lacks granularity. It cannot dis-tinguish a sound, efficient trajectory from one that succeeds by chance and provides no diagnostic signals on failed ex-ecution paths. 

• Reference-matching evaluation measures agent trajecto-ries against a gold standard using exact string matching, token-level n-gram overlap (e.g., BLEU/ROUGE), or se-quential embedding similarity. This imposes a restrictive assumption inherited from chain-of-thought datasets: that a task admits a unique and valid decomposition. In prac-tice, valid plans often diverge in step ordering, granularity, tool selection, or overall strategy. 

• LLM-as-a-judge evaluation (Zheng et al. 2023) provides qualitative flexibility but introduces high computational overhead, non-determinism, extreme prompt sensitivity, and systemic self-preference biases, making it difficult to audit or integrate as a reliable optimization signal. For realistic agentic tasks, there are multiple valid solu-tions, for example those differing by dependency-preserving reorderings, step granularity, or interchangeable tools. Hence agent evaluation metrics should measure closeness to this so-lution space rather than identity with one reference sequence. Because of this reason, we argue that the central evaluation paradigm should shift from checking whether an agent re-produced an exact sequence of reference steps to a holistic graph alignment question: 

How close is the agent’s execution graph to the set of valid solution graphs for the task? 

Answering this question requires trajectory comparison metrics that satisfies a few criteria that non-unique but valid solutions to the same task satisfy: (i) semantic awareness to accommodate paraphrased steps, (ii) permutation tolerance paired with causal strictness to permit independent step re-orderings but penalize true causal inversions, (iii) granularity tolerance to support one-to-many step mappings, (iv) cardi-nality robustness to handle missing or hallucinated steps, (v) 

execution-grounding to incorporate tool environments and state changes, and (vi) multi-reference support to reflect di-verse valid planning strategies. A standard baseline approach might align the agent and arXiv:2607.17082v1 [cs.AI] 19 Jul 2026 reference steps via the Hungarian algorithm (Wright 1990) or greedy assignment over a pairwise embedding cost matrix, similar to token-matching metrics of text similarity (Zhang et al. 2020). However, Hungarian matching is inherently re-stricted to rigid one-to-one correspondences, requires equal cardinalities, and discards causal and sequential graph struc-ture entirely. In this paper, we formalize trajectory evalu-ation through the lens of Optimal Transport (OT) to over-come such issues and satisfy the above six requirements. Our use of unbalanced marginal constraints to absorb cardi-nality mismatches and step variations (Chizat et al. 2018a), soft transport plans naturally capture non-uniform plan gran-ularities, and a fused Gromov-Wasserstein objective directly incorporates relational and causal dependencies into a unified geometric alignment (Titouan et al. 2019). More specifically, 1. We formalize agent trajectory evaluation as a divergence computation between attributed dependency graphs and define criteria for valid trajectory metrics (Sec. 3). 2. We introduce Otap , instantiating this divergence via an unbalanced fused Gromov-Wasserstein (UFGW) formula-tion that couples action, argument, effect, tool, and state-transition attributes with signed dependency structures (Sec. 4). 3. We enable Otap to reference ensembles via a temperature-controlled soft-minimum aggregation, using causal mass weighting to emphasize important path dependencies (Sec. 4.2). 4. We establish theoretical guarantees for Otap , including pseudo-metric structure, invariance under dependency-preserving permutations, bounded sensitivity to redun-dant step insertions, and polynomial computational com-plexity (Sec. 5). 5. We empirically validate Otap on a set of hand-curated tasks and public benchmarks (Sec. 6). 

# 2 Related Work 

Agent benchmarks. Existing evaluation suites like Agent-Bench (Liu et al. 2024), GAIA (Mialon et al. 2024), We-bArena (Zhou et al. 2024), ToolBench (Qin et al. 2024), and 

τ -bench (Yao et al. 2024) primarily emphasize environmen-tal task execution. PlanBench (Valmeekam et al. 2023) uti-lizes formal verification to assess planning accuracy, which provides exact ground truth but remains restricted to highly structured deterministic domains. These benchmarks do not offer a graded, open-ended distance metric between struc-tural execution graphs. Some recent papers propose met-rics beyond accuracy to measure agent performance, such as confidence calibration (Zhang, Xiong, and Wu 2026), uncer-tainty quantification (Tayebati et al. 2026), and consistency measurement (Raj et al. 2026). 

Process-level evaluation. Process reward models (PRMs) evaluate intermediate reasoning steps to optimize mathemat-ical and logical execution (Lightman et al. 2024). However, PRMs require extensive step-level manual annotations and act as black-box learned models. Similarly, LLM-as-a-judge methods (Zheng et al. 2023) inherit high inference costs and non-deterministic variations. In contrast, Otap is determin-istic given text embeddings, is computationally lightweight, and provides explicit sub-loss decompositions. 

Text and structure similarity. Output level lexical met-rics (BLEU, ROUGE) and semantic metrics (Raj, Rosati, and Majumdar 2023; Zhang et al. 2020) alike fail to model trajectory-level nuances such as causal dependencies, macro-level plan structure, or tool environments. Classical graph matchers such as Graph Edit Distance (GED) (Sanfeliu and Fu 1983) do capture topological structure, but are gener-ally NP-hard and lack the capacity to incorporate soft, high-dimensional semantic vector spaces over node attributes. 

Optimal transport. Optimal transport provides a math-ematical foundation for aligning probability distribu-tions (Peyré and Cuturi 2019). Computational efficiency is achieved via entropic regularization and Sinkhorn itera-tions (Cuturi 2013), while unbalanced OT variants relax strict marginal constraints to allow mass modification (Chizat et al. 2018b). The Gromov-Wasserstein (GW) formulation enables alignment between disparate relational structures (Mémoli 2011), and Fused GW extends this to attributed graphs (Vayer et al. 2019). OT methods have been adapted to document sim-ilarity (Kusner et al. 2015) and translation evaluation (Zhao et al. 2019)) in NLP. Otap adapts this mathematical machin-ery to the structured, causal constraints of agent trajectories. 

# 3 Problem Formulation 

Definition 1 (Trajectory graph) . An agent execution trajec-tory is represented as an attributed directed acyclic graph 

G = ( V, E, ϕ ), where each vertex si ∈ V defines an executed step: 

si = ( ai, τ i, c i, e i),

comprising an action description ai, an invoked tool iden-tifier τi ∈ T (where τi = ∅ denotes an internal reasoning step), specific contextual arguments ci, and an execution ef-fect or post-state artifact ei. A directed edge (si, s k) ∈ E

indicates a strict data or causal dependency where step sk

consumes an artifact or requires the prior execution of step 

si. The mapping function ϕ projects step fields into a contin-uous vector space. 

Edges are systematically constructed via three mecha-nisms: (a) extracting explicit dependency graphs from plan-and-execute planners; (b) tracing artifact dependencies in ReAct sequences by linking actions to recent observations; or (c) reverting conservatively to a total execution order when structural dependencies are unrecoverable. Let P = (V P , E P ) denote the agent trajectory with 

|V P | = n, and let R = {R1, . . . , R K } represent a set of diverse reference trajectories where |V Rk | = mk. The eval-uation problem requires computing a normalized distance score score( P, R) ∈ [0 , 1] that faithfully reflects structural and semantic deviations from the valid solution space. 

# 4 The Otap Metric 

We now formalize Optimal Transport Agent Plan ( Otap ) to quantify the similarity between am agent trajectory P and a reference set R. Instead of treating agent trajectories as flat text strings or relying on binary success criteria, Otap 

models execution histories as directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) capturing both semantic content and execution dependencies. 4.1 The optimization problem 

We begin by calculating Otap between two individual tra-jectories (say P, R ) in a three-stage process: (i) computing node-level semantic distances between their individual exe-cution steps, (ii) constructing dependency matrices to capture the topology of the plans, and (iii) solving an OT problem that globally aligns the trajectories.

# 6 Experiments 

We evaluate Otap by comparing it with a range of met-rics (lexical, semantic, and graph-based) in a diverse set of tasks. We hand curate a set of realistic and challeng-ing agentic tasks, as well as use three public benchmarks: PlanBench (Valmeekam et al. 2023), WebArena (Zhou et al. 2024), and GAIA (Mialon et al. 2024). As baseline metrics, we use exact step match, BLEU, and ROUGE-L over concatenated step text, and Otap with the defaults

6.1 Validity 

We apply five controlled perturbations on the canonical refer-ence trajectories in our hand-curated benchmark, then com-pare with a reference set that contains the canonical trajec-tory, a variant in which instructions are paraphrased and steps are reordered, and a coarse variant with merged steps. The perturbations are: (P1) synonym-level paraphrase of all step texts; (P2) an alternative linear extension of the same DAG; 

(P3) merge of two dependent adjacent steps, or split of a compound step into two chained sub-steps; (P4) causal in-version: swapping two steps across a dependency edge, so the consumer executes before its producer; (P5) corruption: deletion of a critical step, insertion of 1-3 irrelevant distractor steps, incompatible tool swaps, or combinations. Agent-side graphs are re-extracted from the perturbed sequences by ar-tifact tracing, so a P4 inversion manifests exactly as it would in a real trace: an unsatisfiable dependency. Ground truth places the original and P1–P3 in one validity tier, P4 strictly below, and P5 at the bottom. 6.3 Ablations 

Table 3 separates the essential from the decorative. 

The structure term is the load-bearing component. Set-ting θ = 0 collapses inversion detection from 70 .7 to 19 .4

and AUROC from 82 .0 to 54 .5: without pairwise dependency geometry, Otap degenerates into a well-dressed unbalanced Wasserstein and inherits the same below-chance pathology as the embedding baselines. Replacing soft transport with Hungarian assignment inside our own fused cost performs no better ( 51 .4 AUROC): the gap between this paper and its seed idea—Hungarian matching on plan steps—is the differ-ence between a working metric and a coin flip. 

Multi-reference aggregation is essential, not garnish. 

A single reference costs 16 .9 AUROC points ( 82 .0 → 65 .1)and halves inversion detection; even with our modest syn-thetic reference diversity, distance-to-a-set is decisively bet-ter than distance-to-a-plan. Aggregation should stay near the minimum: raising the soft-min temperature toward averaging (T = 1 .0) costs 2.5 points. 

Tool grounding and fielded costs each pay for them-selves. Removing the tool term costs 13 .1 AUROC points, concentrated exactly where tools are corrupted (P5: 93 .6 →

68 .4); collapsing the fielded cost to a whole-step embedding costs 9.5 points with the same P5 signature, confirming that separate fields keep opposite actions with shared arguments distinguishable and give the tool term room to act. 

Two components do not earn their complexity, and we say so. Signed versus unsigned dependency distances are in-distinguishable ( 82 .0 vs. 82 .0 AUROC): under artifact trac-ing an inversion deletes the unsatisfiable edge rather than re-versing it, so an ancestor pair becomes incomparable and the magnitude change |DP − DR|2 already fires; the sign could matter only for traces in which a reversed dependency is ex-plicitly recorded. Causal mass weighting is likewise a wash (+1 .7 AUROC, −1.1 ρ against uniform mass); we retain both as options, with uniform unsigned settings a defensible simpler default. 

Balanced marginals degrade gracefully here—but the cardinality gaps are small. Forcing balanced transport costs 

2.8 AUROC points on a corpus whose step counts differ by at most three; larger insertion/deletion regimes should widen this gap, and Prop. 3 is the relevant guarantee. 

An instructive dissociation. The total-order fallback (ix) is the worst configuration for validity discrimination ( 65 .8

AUROC: a chain cannot distinguish valid from invalid re-orderings) yet the best for ladder correlation ( ρ = 0 .949 : a chain registers every perturbation, so its loss tracks raw dam-age count monotonically). Two lessons follow. First, corre-lation with a graded scale is, by itself, a misleading model-selection criterion—it would select a configuration that can-not tell a valid plan from an inverted one; metrics should be selected on the joint (Part A, Part B) evidence. Second, the quality of dependency extraction matters materially: the fall-back degrades discrimination by 16 .2 points—a prediction the GAIA study below confirms on real data. 

6.4 Benchmark evaluations 

The controlled corpus establishes internal validity; we now test transfer on three public benchmarks that differ delib-erately in how much trust each places in the dependency structure and in the labels. 

PlanBench (real LLM plans, oracle labels). The Plan-Bench release (Valmeekam et al. 2023) ships blocksworld plans generated by real LLMs together with VAL-checked validity flags. We convert each LLM plan and the gold plan into trajectory graphs using exact produces/consumes sets from the PDDL operator semantics—dependency DAGs are ground truth here, not heuristic (mechanism (a) of Sec. 3)— and build K=3 genuinely distinct valid references per in-stance with an exact solver (gold plan, an alternative op-timum under a different tie-break, and a state-preserving detour variant). We sample 60 plans per model (balanced across valid/invalid where possible) for 12 models spanning GPT-3.5/4/4o, Claude 3/3.5, Gemini, Llama-3.1, and Qwen families: 720 real trajectories. Our re-implemented validity checker agrees with the shipped VAL labels on 720 /720 

plans. Two studies result: (a) an oracle study —per-metric AUROC of the score against the validity label, the “external check at zero annotation cost”; and (b) the P1–P5/Q1–Q5 pro-tocol run on 20 gold plans ( 520 trajectories), i.e. controlled ground truth over real-domain plans with exact dependency semantics. 

GAIA (real human-authored solutions, heuristic ex-traction). Every GAIA record (Mialon et al. 2024) carries an annotator-written step-by-step solution. Oracle study: an honest negative result. Table 4 shows that on the task of predicting formal plan validity, every reference-proximity metric is a decent proxy ( 79 –87 AU-ROC over real LLM outputs)—and Otap is the weakest of them, 7.5 points behind BLEU. We report this prominently because it delimits the method’s scope. The oracle asks a dif-ferent question than the one Otap is built to answer: validity in blocksworld is a global executability property of a near-unique optimal plan, so any deviation from the reference— extra steps, missing steps, granularity drift—is evidence of invalidity, and the strictest surface metrics profit from punish-ing all of it. Otap is designed to forgive exactly those devia-tions (unbalanced marginals absorb insertions and deletions; soft transport absorbs granularity), and that deliberate tol-erance costs discrimination when tolerance is unwarranted. The practical reading is unambiguous: when a formal checker exists, use the checker—no proximity metric, ours included, should substitute for it. Reference-based process metrics are for the open-ended domains where no verifier exists, and the perturbation results below measure that regime directly.

With heuristic structure, the advantage persists but shrinks, and we can quantify the cost. On GAIA, Otap still has the best discrimination by a wide margin ( 69 .8 AUROC; no baseline exceeds 58 .5, and four of five are at or below chance), and it is the only metric above 50 on all three be-nign families—the token metrics rank every valid reordering below every corruption (BLEU and ROUGE-L P2 = 0 .0). But the noisy extraction exacts a measurable toll on Otap 

itself: P2 falls from the exact 100 .0 of every clean-structure corpus to 67 .3, severity ordering drops to 68 .9, and the ladder correlation collapses to ρ = 0 .53 while the order-blind em-bedding baselines, immune to edge noise by construction, stay high ( 0.82 –0.86 ). The mechanism is the one ablation (ix) predicted: when salient-token tracing mistakes coinci-dental lexical overlap for a dependency, a valid reordering perturbs the extracted DP and is charged for structure it did not break. The controlled ablation put the extraction-quality cost at 16 .2 AUROC points; GAIA confirms on real human-authored plans that dependency extraction is the binding constraint for structural metrics in the wild. 

6.5 Runtime 

On CPU, one Otap evaluation of a six-step trajectory against 

K=3 references—including all Sinkhorn and conditional-gradient iterations, excluding encoder cache warm-up— takes 28 –33 ms across the controlled, PlanBench, and We-bArena corpora, against 0.006 –22 ms for the baselines. Cost is not flat in trajectory length: on GAIA’s longer human-authored solutions the quadratic Gromov–Wasserstein term raises the mean to 23 .9 s per evaluation (ROUGE-L’s LCS suffers similarly, at 180 s), consistent with the complexity analysis of Sec. 5; sparse structural distances and early-stopped outer iterations are the natural mitigations for long-horizon traces. In the short-horizon regime Otap remains negligible next to an LLM-judge call, which costs seconds of latency and per-token fees and is non-deterministic; Otap 

is deterministic, offline, and cheap enough to run inside a training loop as a process reward. 

6.6 Human evaluation 

Validating a metric requires trajectories whose relative qual-ity is known. Our evidence therefore comes in two tiers. The first is a fully automatic, reproducible protocol in which ground-truth quality orderings hold by construction : a cor-pus of plan DAGs is subjected to controlled perturbations of known severity, and a metric is measured by how often it recovers the resulting ordering (Part A) and by its corre-lation with a graded corruption ladder (Part B). The second tier moves to three public benchmarks: PlanBench, where 

720 real LLM-generated plans carry oracle validity labels at zero annotation cost; and GAIA and WebArena, where the perturbation protocol is re-run on real human-authored solutions and real task intents, respectively. Across all stud-ies ( 3,092 trajectories in total) we use a single fixed seed and the a priori default hyper-parameters of Sec. 4; no tun-ing of any kind was performed on any corpus. We release the full harness—corpora, loaders, perturbation generator, all metric implementations, and seeds—for one-command reproduction. 

# 7 Conclusion 

In this paper, we have proposed Otap as a granular metric for evaluating agent trajectories that account for their het-erogenous graph structure and multiple reference solutions. Beyond evaluation, the differentiable structure of (3) makes 

Otap a candidate dense process reward, and the soft-min over references points toward learned models of the solution manifold. The experiments reveal two open problems that we leave for future work: dependency extraction from free-text traces, and principled granularity invariance. References
